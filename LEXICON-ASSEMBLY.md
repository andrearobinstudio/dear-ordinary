# Assembling the Lexicon
## Visual Language of Queering Contemplation — Process Documentation

*Part of the Dear Ordinary: Queering Contemplation through Art Practice thesis inquiry*

## What the lexicon is

The lexicon is a growing collection of images — drawn from the historical and contemporary artists studied across 56 weeks — organised by the visual element they are being held *for*. Not "this is a Morandi" but "this is what a restricted palette held with fidelity looks like." Not "this is Käthe Kollwitz" but "this is what mark-as-nervous-system-contact looks like."

The artist is the source. The visual element is the category. The image is the evidence.

Across 56 weeks, this collection becomes a visual language: a living record of what queering contemplation has looked like in the hands of makers who came before, distilled into the elements that are available to be spoken in new combinations in original work.

---

## Two forms: physical and digital

The lexicon is maintained in two parallel forms that serve different cognitive functions. Both are kept current. Neither replaces the other.

### The physical wall (or rolling board)

A dedicated physical surface — wall space or a rolling pinboard — organised into zones by visual element category. Images are printed and pinned. Small adhesive tags indicate secondary categories (see tagging, below).

The physical form does what screens cannot: it lets you see the whole field at once, without scrolling. Unexpected adjacencies emerge. A colour from Week 3 sits next to one from Week 34 and something becomes visible that neither held alone. The body is in the room with the material. The physical lexicon is for seeing, feeling, and being surprised.

### Figma (digital)

A single Figma file — `visual-lexicon.fig` — structured as described below. This is the searchable, archivable, thesis-ready record. It is also the source for printing: images are placed here first, then printed for the wall.

The digital form does what a physical wall cannot: it is searchable by tag, reproducible, portable, and permanent. It holds the text notes attached to each image. It is the form that travels to the thesis.

## The Figma file structure

### Pages

One page per visual element category, plus two special pages:

| Page | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **00 · All entries** | Every image in chronological order by week. The accumulation as timeline. |
| **01 · Colour** | Palette, temperature, saturation, value relationships, near-disappearance, luminosity |
| **02 · Mark** | Stroke quality, pressure, hesitation, accumulation, discharge, duration-as-mark |
| **03 · Composition** | Spatial organisation, edge/centre decisions, how the eye is asked to move or rest |
| **04 · Light** | Source, direction, quality, what it falls on, revelation vs concealment |
| **05 · Subject** | What bodies, objects, relationships, or moments are treated as worthy of attention |
| **06 · Scale & gesture** | What size asks of the encountering body; gesture across scales |
| **07 · Process logic** | Duration, repetition, accumulation, subtraction, chance, seriality, material resistance |
| **08 · Structural moves** | Operations that could be translated across media — holding rupture, attending until infinite, making absence present |
| **09 · [New category]** | Added as discovered — see expanding the lexicon below |

### Each entry: the card format

Every image in Figma lives in a card. The card contains:

```
[ IMAGE — the specific work or detail that resonated ]

Artist name · Week ##
Primary category: [e.g. Mark]
Also tagged: [e.g. Composition, Light]

What resonated:
One or two sentences. Specific. Not "the grief" but "the way the brushstroke 
presses and releases within a single mark, as if the hand is both holding 
and letting go simultaneously."
```

The image should be the *specific work or detail* that carried the resonance — not a representative work by the artist, not a portrait of the artist. If it was a detail of a larger painting, crop to that detail. What resonated is what gets captured.

### Tagging

Each entry has one primary category (the page it lives on) and any number of secondary tags. Secondary tags appear on the card and allow cross-referencing without duplication. An image of Morandi's bottles might live on **01 · Colour** with secondary tags `Composition` and `Process logic`. It does not appear on those other pages, but a search or filter for those tags will find it.

In Figma, secondary tags are added as small text labels across the bottom of the card, visually distinct from the primary category label.

## The physical wall setup

### Zones

Divide the wall or board into zones matching the Figma pages — one zone per category, labelled. Leave room for growth: each zone should start with space to expand.

An **All entries** strip runs along the top or bottom edge in chronological order — every printed image appears here as a small thumbnail, giving a visual timeline of the accumulation.

### Each printed card

Print at a consistent size — A5 or 4×6" works well for a wall you can read from standing distance. Each card shows:

- The image
- Artist name · Week ##
- Primary category (printed or written)
- Secondary tags as small coloured dot stickers (one colour per category, key posted on the wall)

### Colour dot key (suggested)

| Dot colour | Category |
|---|---|
| No dot | Primary category only |
| Red | Colour |
| Black | Mark |
| Blue | Composition |
| Yellow | Light |
| Green | Subject |
| Orange | Scale & gesture |
| Purple | Process logic |
| White | Structural moves |

Add new dot colours as new categories emerge.

## Weekly workflow

Each week, after studying the historical and contemporary artist pair:

1. **Notice what resonated** — a specific work, or a specific detail within a work. Not the artist's practice in general; the particular image that carried something.

2. **Identify the visual element** — what is it being held for? Name the primary category and any secondary ones.

3. **Write the "what resonated" note** — one or two sentences. Specific. This is the most important step: the note is what makes the image a lexicon entry rather than a bookmark.

4. **Add to Figma first** — place the image, complete the card, add tags.

5. **Print and pin** — print the card, add coloured dot tags, pin to the correct zone on the wall. Add a small thumbnail to the All Entries strip.

6. **Record in the week file** — the Artist Influence Log section of `week-##.md` captures the same resonance in words; the lexicon captures it in image. Both records are made.

## Expanding the lexicon

The starter categories cover the visual elements most likely to carry meaning in this inquiry. New categories will emerge — perhaps **Texture**, or **Gesture-as-theology**, or **The body in the frame**, or something that doesn't yet have a name.

When a resonance doesn't fit an existing category:

1. Add it to the **All entries** page with a note: *[possible new category: ___]*
2. After three or more entries suggest the same new category, formalise it: add a new page in Figma, a new zone on the wall, a new dot colour.
3. Document the addition in the **Lexicon notes** section at the bottom of this file.

The lexicon's categories are themselves a finding. What categories were needed, which expanded, which collapsed into each other — that record is part of the thesis evidence.

---

## The lexicon as thesis evidence

By Week 56, the lexicon holds up to 112 entries (two artists per week) and a set of categories refined through use. This is not a mood board or an inspiration archive. It is a systematically assembled record of what queering contemplation looks like visually across artists, traditions, and centuries — and a demonstration, in practice, of how a visual language is built through sustained, respectful attention to those who made before.

The lexicon will be presented in the thesis as:
- Evidence for the hypothesis (the practice was genuinely non-linear, genuinely engaged, genuinely accumulated)
- A methodology document (this is how visual knowledge is built)
- A visual appendix (the Figma file, or selected pages from it, as documentation of the inquiry)
