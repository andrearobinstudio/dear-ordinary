# Week 18 · The sacred is found in the bodies of the marginalized — not symbolically but actually, and this changes everything

**Phase:** III — The Mystical Thread
**Constellation:** The Engaged Path
**Star:** Liberation theology

---

## Grounding: The preferential option for the poor

Gustavo Gutiérrez's liberation theology — emerging from the crucible of Latin American poverty — established the claim that has since been elaborated by James Cone, Marcella Althaus-Reid, and many others: God is found among those who suffer. Not as a comfort to suffering but as a presence that transforms the suffering into a site of revelation. Cone's Black Theology of Liberation insists that the God of the Exodus is on the side of those being enslaved, not the slaveholders — and this is not a marginal claim but the tradition's most central one, recovered after centuries of being covered over.

**Key sentence:** Liberation theology is not a political distortion of theology — it is the recovery of theology's most ancient claim: that the sacred is encountered precisely where the dominant culture least expects it, in the bodies of those the system has cast aside.

---

## Ethics: Althaus-Reid's indecent theology and the body that refuses to be sanitized

Marcella Althaus-Reid's *Indecent Theology* goes further than most liberation theology: honest theology must begin from the body as it actually is, not as institution demands. The lemon sellers of Buenos Aires — poor, female, sexualized, making their living at the margins — are the starting point, not a deviation from the proper theological subject. Indecent theology refuses to sanitize the body, desire, or politics in the name of an acceptable sacred. It is, at its core, a queer theological move.

**Key sentence:** Any theology that begins from an idealized body rather than the actual, marked, desiring, struggling body has already made a political choice — and it is the choice that second-satan systems have always made.

---

## Emergence: The kitchen table as a theological location

Ada María Isasi-Díaz's mujerista theology introduces *lo cotidiano* — the everyday — as the locus of theological reflection. The kitchen table, the community meeting, the ordinary day: these are not the backdrop to theological life but its primary site. This is the framework's claim about the ordinary made explicit: *Dear Ordinary* is a form of address precisely because the ordinary is already holy, already the site of encounter, already the place where the sacred is doing its most important work.

**Key sentence:** The ordinary is not the waiting room before the sacred arrives — the ordinary is the sacred's primary address, and the body that lives the ordinary life is already the body that theological reflection must begin from.
