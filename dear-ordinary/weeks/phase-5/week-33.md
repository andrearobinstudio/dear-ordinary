# Week 33 · The mystics were often queerer than their institutions — and the institution's management of their work has been covering this over for centuries

**Phase:** V — The Wide Waters
**Constellation:** The Queer Path
**Star:** Queer mysticism

---

## Grounding: Rumi, John of the Cross, the Song of Songs

The erotic language of the mystical tradition has always exceeded heteronormative containment. Rumi's Masnavi is saturated with same-sex desire and longing — frequently sanitized in Western translation. John of the Cross writes with an eroticism that the tradition has managed by calling it 'allegorical.' The Song of Songs, with its unabashed erotic poetry, has required centuries of allegorical interpretation to make it theologically acceptable. Mark Jordan's Foucault-inflected reading shows that contemplative practice has been used both to discipline and to liberate desire — and that the disciplining function has been the institution's primary use, while the liberating function belongs to the mystics themselves.

**Key sentence:** The mystical tradition's use of erotic language — same-sex desire, bodily longing, the dissolution of the boundary between lover and beloved — has always exceeded the institution's management of it, and this excess is the tradition's most honest part.

---

## Ethics: Patrick Cheng and the theological dissolution of boundaries

Patrick Cheng's *Radical Love* argues that queer theology's dissolution of fixed identity boundaries mirrors contemplative non-dual awareness: the queer experience of being neither/both, of refusing fixed categories, of living in the between, is a form of preparation for and expression of the mystical dissolution of the subject-object split. Queer desire — desire that does not fit the categories the system recognizes — is not a problem for theology but one of its most important teachers.

**Key sentence:** Queer desire — precisely because it does not fit the institution's categories — is one of the most reliable teachers of the apophatic posture: the experience of the self exceeding every definition that others have tried to impose.

---

## Emergence: The erotic as contemplative and the contemplative as erotic

Audre Lorde and John of the Cross, unlikely companions, arrive at a shared territory: the erotic and the contemplative are not separate domains but the same depth of experience approached from different angles. The capacity for genuine erotic feeling — feeling in its deepest sense, the felt sense of what is genuinely alive — is the same capacity that makes genuine contemplation possible. The systems that deaden the erotic deaden the contemplative. The recovery of the erotic is the recovery of contemplative depth.

**Key sentence:** The recovery of erotic depth — the capacity to feel what is genuinely alive — is the recovery of contemplative depth, and the systems that police desire are policing the very capacity that makes genuine prayer possible.
