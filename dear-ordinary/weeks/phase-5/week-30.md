# Week 30 · Exile from any second-satan system is not a spiritual dead end — the wilderness is where the divine is most honestly encountered

**Phase:** V — The Wide Waters
**Constellation:** The Queer Path
**Star:** Exile as contemplative location

---

## Grounding: The wilderness as theological site

John Fortunato's *Embracing the Exile* reframes what LGBTQ+ people who have been pushed to the margins of religious institutions have known: exile is not a failure state but a contemplative location. The wilderness — the place outside the city walls, outside the institution, outside the system of acceptable identity — is where, in the biblical tradition, the divine is consistently encountered most honestly. Moses in the desert. Elijah under the broom tree. Jesus in the wilderness for forty days. The margin is not the edge of the sacred; it is one of its most reliable addresses.

**Key sentence:** The exile knows things the insider cannot — not because exile is better than belonging, but because the view from the margin is unobstructed by the institution's management of what can be seen.

---

## Ethics: The theological significance of the margin

The insider's relationship with the institution is mediated by the institution's need to maintain its own legitimacy; the exile's relationship with the sacred is unmediated by that need. This is not romanticization of suffering; it is a structural observation. The person who has been told by the institution that they are unacceptable to the sacred, and who has continued to encounter the sacred anyway — often more immediately, more urgently, more honestly than before the rejection — knows something the institution cannot afford to acknowledge.

**Key sentence:** The insider who has never been exiled cannot know what the exile knows — that the sacred does not require the institution's permission to show up — and this knowledge, once gained, permanently reorganizes the practitioner's relationship to every institution.

---

## Emergence: The studio as a non-institutional sacred space

The art studio, for many practitioners, functions as the non-institutional sacred space — the place outside the system's jurisdiction where genuine encounter becomes possible without requiring the system's permission. This is not merely convenient; it is theologically significant. The studio that is genuinely not beholden to any market, any institution, any criterion of acceptable identity is a form of exile that has become habitable — which is what Fortunato means by embracing it.

**Key sentence:** The studio that is genuinely outside institutional jurisdiction — that does not need the market's approval, the institution's blessing, or the critic's validation — is a form of exile made habitable, and it is a genuinely contemplative space precisely because of, not despite, its marginality.

## Art Practice Invitation

Make something in an unusual location — outside, in a stairwell, in a space that is not a studio. Let the non-institutional space do something to the making. Notice what changes when the system's jurisdiction is absent.
