# Week 23 · There is no separate self that could be saved or damned alone — the ecological and the contemplative are one movement

**Phase:** IV — The Engaged Path
**Constellation:** The Wide Waters
**Star:** Interbeing

---

## Grounding: The radical interdependence of all phenomena

Thich Nhat Hanh's concept of interbeing — *interêtre* — is a contemplative claim as much as a scientific one: nothing exists independently of everything else. The flower contains the cloud, the sunshine, the soil, the farmer, the death of other living things. Arne Naess's deep ecology expands the boundary of identity to include the living world: the mature ecological self is not the individual human bounded by skin, but the process of life in which each individual participates. David Abram's phenomenology grounds this in the body's actual perceptual experience: we are already in relation with the animate Earth before any concept of independence arises.

**Key sentence:** Interbeing is not a spiritual aspiration but an accurate description of what is already the case — the illusion is not interdependence but the bounded, separate self that the second-satan system requires in order to police it.

---

## Ethics: Who bears the cost of the fiction of the separate self

The fiction of the separate, self-sufficient individual — which underlies both Western individualism and its theological equivalent, the soul who answers for itself alone — has historically distributed its costs unevenly. Communities, particularly indigenous communities, poor communities, and communities of color, have always known what the dominant culture denies: survival is communal, healing is communal, wisdom is communal. The second-satan system's insistence on individual pathology serves its need to locate the wound in the person rather than the structure.

**Key sentence:** The fiction of the separate self serves those who benefit from structures that harm others — because if the problem is always individual, the structure is never required to change.

---

## Emergence: Making together as interbeing in practice

Communal making — sacred chant, communal weaving, liturgical dance, the Taizé model — is not merely making with other people. It is a practice of interbeing: the dissolution of the individual creative will into a shared creative process that produces something none of the individuals could have produced alone. Ellen Dissanayake's research on what art is for argues that communal making is one of its most ancient and most important functions — a technology for producing the felt sense of belonging to something larger.

**Key sentence:** Making together is a practice of interbeing — not the individual genius producing a solitary work, but the communal body discovering what emerges when individual will is released into shared attention.

## Art Practice Invitation

Make something with at least one other person, surrendering individual control of the outcome. Bring genuine attention to the experience of co-creation — where your contribution ends and theirs begins, and what happens in that threshold.
