# Week 26 · The grief of the body is the body completing what the loss interrupted — and it is sacred work

**Phase:** IV — The Engaged Path
**Constellation:** The Dark Night
**Star:** Grief as sacred work

---

## Grounding: John of the Cross and the necessary passage

John of the Cross wrote from prison, from exile, from the experience of being persecuted by the institution he had devoted his life to. His *Dark Night of the Soul* is not a theoretical document; it is a map drawn from the inside. The dark night, he insists, is not punishment — it is the soul being freed from attachments that cannot hold the weight of love. Peter Levine's somatic work adds the biological complement: grief is the body completing the response to loss, the discharge of what was mobilized when the loss occurred. Womanist theology names the political dimension: grief is the refusal to pretend systemic wounds did not happen.

**Key sentence:** Grief is sacred work — not because suffering is sacred in itself, but because genuine grief is the body's honest response to genuine loss, and honoring it is a form of truth-telling that the system consistently requires us to suppress.

---

## Ethics: Who is permitted to grieve, and for how long

The permission to grieve is not equally distributed. LGBTQ+ people who lose partners, friends, or community members often grieve without the social recognition that names the loss as real — disenfranchised grief, as the literature calls it. The grief of those whose lives the dominant culture has designated as less valuable is consistently minimized, hurried, or denied. And the political grief — the grief for what the system has taken, the years lost to shame, the relationships destroyed by religious rejection — rarely has a name or a container.

**Key sentence:** Disenfranchised grief — grief that the dominant culture refuses to name, recognize, or give time for — is one of the specific harms that second-satan systems inflict, and naming it is part of metabolizing it.

---

## Emergence: Grief as politically necessary refusal

Womanist theology — particularly Delores Williams and Emilie Townes — insists that grief is politically necessary for those whose communities have been systematically harmed: the refusal to pretend the wounds did not happen, the insistence on naming the loss, the commitment to not forgetting. This is grief as a form of integrity, not a form of weakness. The art practice has historically been one of the most important containers for this grief — making visible what the culture requires to remain invisible.

**Key sentence:** Grief named, held, and expressed — rather than managed, minimized, or suppressed — is a form of political integrity as much as personal healing, and the art practice is one of its most ancient and important containers.
