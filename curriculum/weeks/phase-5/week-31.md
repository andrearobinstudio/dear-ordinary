# Week 31 · The particular, marked, desiring body is not an obstacle to contemplative life — it is its very site and medium

**Phase:** V — The Wide Waters
**Constellation:** The Queer Path
**Star:** The body as theological site

---

## Grounding: Queer theology's most consistent gift

Isherwood and Stuart's *Introducing Body Theology*, Althaus-Reid's *Indecent Theology*, Scott Kugle's *Sufis and Saints' Bodies* — these texts converge on what queer theology's most consistent contribution to the broader contemplative tradition has been: the insistence that the particular, marked, desiring, gendered, racialized body is not an obstacle to contemplative life but its very site and medium. Kugle's research on Sufi saints shows that holiness was never disembodied — the saints' bodies, in all their particularity, were themselves the site of mystical encounter. The body that the institution has judged is the body in which the sacred is most honestly encountered.

**Key sentence:** Queer theology's most radical claim — that the particular, marked body is not the obstacle to the sacred but its primary medium — is also its most ancient claim, recovered from traditions the institution has suppressed.

---

## Ethics: Judith Butler and the bodies that matter

Judith Butler's *Bodies That Matter* asks which bodies are granted the status of mattering — of being real, of being grievable, of being recognized as sites of genuine experience. The bodies the institution has deemed disordered, wrong, excessive, or unacceptable are precisely the bodies that queer theology insists matter most — not because suffering confers dignity, but because the sacred cannot be confined to the bodies the institution finds acceptable. This is a claim about reality, not about idealism.

**Key sentence:** Any theology that confines the sacred to bodies the institution finds acceptable has already limited its God to the institution's preferences — and this is a theological error as much as a political one.

---

## Emergence: The artist's body as theological site

The art practice that is genuinely somatic — that involves the body as the primary instrument of making, not merely the hand executing the mind's instructions — is a practice of queer body theology: insisting that this particular body, in its particularity, is a site of sacred encounter. Authentic Movement, somatic art practice, the work of the hands with resistant materials — these are practices that locate the sacred in the body's actual experience rather than in its performance of acceptability.

**Key sentence:** The art practice that locates the sacred in the body's actual experience — its weight, its sensation, its particular way of moving through the world — is practicing queer body theology whether or not it names itself as such.

## Art Practice Invitation

Make something using your body as the primary instrument — not just the hand. Use the whole body: lean into the canvas, use both hands, use weight and breath. Make something that could only be made by this particular body.
