# Week 05 · Contemplative practice can harm — and this is not a reason to abandon it but a reason to begin it honestly

**Phase:** I — The Body's Wisdom  
**Constellation:** The Body's Wisdom  
**Star:** The difficult side of practice

---

## Grounding: What the research shows

Willoughby Britton's research at Cheetah House, and David Treleaven's trauma-sensitive mindfulness framework, document what many practitioners already know from experience: contemplative practice can destabilize. Depersonalization, increased anxiety, intrusive memories, psychotic episodes — these are documented outcomes for some practitioners, particularly those carrying unresolved trauma. The contemplative tradition has always known that descent precedes ascent, that the dark night is real. Modern research names what the tradition knew: the territory is not safe, and a map that pretends otherwise is not a spiritual map.

**Key sentence:** The difficult side of contemplative practice is not an unfortunate side effect to be managed — it is part of the map, and any practice that hides it is incomplete.

---

## Ethics: Who is most at risk, and from what?

The practitioners most at risk from destabilizing contemplative experiences are disproportionately those already carrying second-satan wounds: people whose bodies have been pathologized, whose interiority has been surveilled, whose sense of self has been systematically assaulted. A meditation tradition that offers the same practice to everyone without attending to this differential risk is not neutral — it replicates the logic of the systems it claims to counter. Trauma-sensitive mindfulness is not a luxury addition for special cases; it is the ethical minimum.

**Key sentence:** No spiritual bypassing — the difficult is part of the map, and those most likely to encounter it are those whose bodies the systems have already put at greatest risk.

---

## Emergence: Titration, not transcendence

The response to the difficult side of practice is not to abandon practice, nor to push through at any cost, but to titrate: to work in small doses, with resources, with the capacity to orient back to safety. This is what the window of tolerance teaches. The art practice offers particular gifts here — the studio can be entered and left, the making can be paused, the material holds what the maker cannot yet hold alone. Process over product is not a slogan; it is a trauma-informed principle.

**Key sentence:** The practice that knows how to stop — that can titrate, pause, orient — is more trustworthy than the practice that demands you push through, and more likely to lead somewhere genuinely transformative.

## Art Practice Invitation

Begin a piece. Deliberately stop before you feel you should. Put it down. Come back to it after a day. Notice what happens in the interval — in the body, in the work. Practice stopping as a contemplative act.
