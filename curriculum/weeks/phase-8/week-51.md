# Week 51 · The methodology section is the place where the thesis tells the truth about how it knows what it claims to know

**Phase:** VIII — Synthesis
**Constellation:** Investigation
**Star:** Thesis structure: Methodology

---

## Grounding: Method as epistemology

The methodology section of a thesis is not merely a description of what the researcher did; it is an account of the epistemological commitments that made the research possible. For a practice-based thesis that combines scholarly writing and visual art, the methodology section must do something that most methodology sections do not: account for two distinct but related forms of inquiry, and explain how knowledge is produced by each, how they relate to each other, and what the combined methodology produces that neither alone could. This is a demanding methodological task, and it is also an opportunity: the methodology section is where the thesis's queering of academic convention is most explicit.

**Key sentence:** The methodology section is not a procedural account of what was done — it is the thesis's epistemological declaration: an account of how it knows what it claims to know, and why that knowing is trustworthy.

---

## Ethics: The ethics of autoethnography

A practice-based thesis that centers the practitioner's own experience is, in significant part, autoethnographic: the researcher's body, history, and practice are the primary research site. Autoethnography carries specific ethical responsibilities: the obligation to attend to positionality (who is doing the research, from what location, with what investments), the obligation to protect others who appear in the research, and the obligation to be genuinely honest about what the research can and cannot claim. For a thesis explicitly located in queer experience, these obligations are particularly acute — the researcher is not a neutral observer but a particular body with a particular history in relation to the systems the thesis names.

**Key sentence:** The autoethnographic practitioner-researcher has specific ethical obligations — to positionality, to honesty about limits, to the protection of others — that the methodology section must address explicitly, because these obligations are not incidental to the research but constitutive of it.

---

## Emergence: Validity in practice-based research

If validity in empirical research is a matter of whether the methods actually measure what they claim to measure, validity in practice-based research is a matter of different criteria: resonance (does the account ring true to others with similar experience?), transferability (can the findings be applied to other contexts?), and reflexivity (has the researcher accounted honestly for their own position?). These are not lesser criteria; they are appropriate to the kind of knowledge the research produces. The methodology section that accounts for these criteria honestly is stronger than one that tries to mimic empirical standards.

**Key sentence:** Validity in practice-based research is not measured by the standards of empirical research — it is measured by resonance, transferability, and reflexivity, and a methodology section that applies these criteria honestly is more trustworthy than one that tries to claim empirical validity for a fundamentally different kind of inquiry.
