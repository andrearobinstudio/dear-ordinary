# Week 37 · What distinguishes contemplative art practice from art-making in general is not the medium but the quality of attention

**Phase:** VI — The Wide Waters / The Making
**Constellation:** The Making
**Star:** Making as praying

---

## Grounding: Attention as the distinguishing quality

Shaun McNiff's *Art as Medicine*, Pat Allen's open studio practice, John Daido Loori's *The Zen of Creativity* — these practitioners converge on a claim that sounds simple and is actually demanding: what distinguishes contemplative art practice from art-making in general is not the subject matter, the medium, the tradition, or the skill level. It is the quality of attention brought to the process. The contemplative artist is not primarily making an object but cultivating a state of presence. McNiff's insistence that the image has its own healing intelligence — that the practitioner's task is to listen to the image rather than impose on it — is an apophatic move applied to art-making: releasing the ego's agenda in favor of what the work itself wants to become.

**Key sentence:** Contemplative art practice and general art-making use the same materials and produce similar objects — the difference is entirely in the quality of attention brought to the process, and this difference is everything.

---

## Ethics: The market's demands on attention

The art market — like every second-satan system — colonizes attention. It requires the artist to make what will sell, to develop a recognizable style, to produce toward a predetermined outcome, to brand the creative self. This is not merely a pragmatic constraint; it is a spiritual one: the artist who must always be calculating toward the market's response cannot simultaneously be genuinely present to what the work wants to become. The open studio practice is a form of resistance to this colonization — a space where the work is not produced for any market, where the attention is genuinely free.

**Key sentence:** The art market's demand that the artist make toward a predetermined outcome — toward what will sell, what will be recognized, what will build the brand — is a colonization of attention that forecloses the very quality that makes art-making contemplative.

---

## Emergence: Making and praying are not always different activities

Julia Cameron's *The Artist's Way*, however commercially successful, holds something genuinely important: the creative practice as a spiritual practice, the studio as the site of encounter with something larger than the ego's agenda. Loori's Zen framing is more rigorous: creativity is itself a gate of Zen practice, not an illustration of it. The distinction between the sacred and the creative collapses when the attention is genuine enough. This is not mystification of art-making; it is the most honest description of what genuine making actually feels like from the inside.

**Key sentence:** When the attention is genuine enough — when the ego's agenda is genuinely released and the practitioner is genuinely present to what wants to emerge — making and praying are not always different activities.
