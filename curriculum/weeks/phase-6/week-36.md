# Week 36 · The ecological crisis and the spiritual crisis are the same crisis — and both require the same healing

**Phase:** VI — The Wide Waters / The Making
**Constellation:** The Wide Waters
**Star:** Interbeing — synthesis

---

## Grounding: What queer ecology names

The ecological crisis — the accelerating destruction of living systems, the sixth mass extinction, the warming planet — is not a separate problem from the spiritual crisis that this curriculum addresses. Catriona Sandilands and Bruce Erickson's queer ecology scholarship names the connection precisely: heteronormativity and ecological domination share the same conceptual root. The logic that naturalizes the domination of queer bodies is the logic that naturalizes the domination of the Earth. Thomas Berry's 'new story' — the cosmological narrative in which the Earth is a community of subjects rather than a collection of objects — is the alternative to both, simultaneously.

**Key sentence:** The ecological and the contemplative are one movement — and a spirituality that heals the person while ignoring the systems that are destroying the Earth has not yet fully understood what it is healing.

---

## Ethics: Who bears the ecological burden

The ecological crisis, like every crisis, distributes its harm unevenly. Indigenous communities, poor communities, communities of color bear disproportionate ecological burden while having contributed least to the conditions that produced it. Environmental justice and queer justice are not separate movements with separate theories; they are expressions of the same refusal of extractive logic. The second-satan system that extracts from queer bodies is the same system that extracts from the Earth — and the movements that resist these extractions belong together.

**Key sentence:** Environmental justice and queer justice are not parallel movements — they are the same movement resisting the same extractive logic operating in different domains, and the contemplative practice that addresses one without the other is incomplete.

---

## Emergence: Art as ecological relationship

The art practice, when it takes seriously the ecological origin of its materials, the ecological cost of its production, and the ecological imagination it can cultivate, becomes a form of ecological contemplation. Anna Tsing's work on multispecies entanglement, Robin Wall Kimmerer's *Braiding Sweetgrass*, the wider tradition of land art and ecological art — these point toward a making practice that is in relationship with the Earth rather than extractive from it. The studio that tends to its materials as gifts rather than resources is practicing ecological contemplation.

**Key sentence:** The art practice that tends to its materials as gifts from living systems — that makes in relationship with the Earth rather than extracting from it — is practicing ecological contemplation, and this is one of the most concrete forms the framework's widest claim takes.

## Art Practice Invitation

Make something using only materials that are gifts from the natural world — fallen leaves, water, mud, stone, found wood. Attend to the ecological biography of each material: where it came from, what it is part of. Let the material's origin be part of the work.
