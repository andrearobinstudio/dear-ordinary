# Week 40 · Making together is a form of being together in the presence of something larger than any individual creative will

**Phase:** VI — The Wide Waters / The Making
**Constellation:** The Making
**Star:** Making together as sacred assembly

---

## Grounding: The communal origins of art-making

Ellen Dissanayake's *What Is Art For?* argues from evolutionary anthropology that art's most ancient function is communal: the making of what is special, together, in contexts of shared attention and shared meaning-making. Sacred chant, communal weaving, liturgical dance, the Taizé model of communal song — these are not exceptions to the normal individual practice of art but representations of art's original and most important form. Natalie Rogers's person-centered expressive arts and the work of Paolo Knill in expressive arts therapy both demonstrate that communal making produces effects that individual making does not: the felt sense of belonging, the regulation that co-presence produces, the creativity that emerges from the friction and harmony of multiple bodies making together.

**Key sentence:** Making together is not a social version of individual making — it is a qualitatively different activity that produces what individual making cannot: the experience of belonging to something larger than the individual creative will.

---

## Ethics: Who has been excluded from the making communities

The history of art institutions is, in significant part, a history of exclusion: from the academies that excluded women, to the galleries that excluded artists of color, to the critical frameworks that excluded outsider art, to the art schools that excluded those without economic access. This exclusion is not incidental; it serves the function of keeping the making community homogeneous, keeping value concentrated, keeping the definition of what counts as art in the hands of those who benefit from the exclusion. The open studio, the community art practice, the expressive arts group — these are not lesser alternatives to the serious art world; they are forms of reclaiming a function that was always communal.

**Key sentence:** The exclusion of marginal bodies from making communities is not a failure of inclusion — it is a deliberate function of the institutions that benefit from keeping the definition of art in the hands of those who already hold power.

---

## Emergence: Sacred assembly in the studio

The Taizé model — simple, repetitive, communal singing that creates a shared contemplative state across cultural and denominational boundaries — offers a template for communal making: accessible, genuinely non-hierarchical, focused on the quality of shared presence rather than the quality of individual performance. The art studio that creates genuine conditions for sacred assembly — in which everyone's contribution is genuinely welcomed, in which the focus is on shared attention rather than individual achievement — is practicing the most ancient function of art.

**Key sentence:** The studio that genuinely welcomes every body's contribution — that focuses on shared attention rather than individual achievement, communal presence rather than hierarchical evaluation — is practicing art's most ancient and most important function.

## Art Practice Invitation

Make something collaboratively — with one or more people. Surrender individual control of the outcome. Attend to the experience of co-creation: what is lost when you share control, and what becomes possible that was impossible alone.
