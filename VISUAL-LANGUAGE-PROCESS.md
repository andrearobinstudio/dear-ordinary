# Building a Visual Language of Queering Contemplation
## Process Documentation — Andrea Robin Studio

*Part of the Dear Ordinary: Queering Contemplation through Art Practice thesis inquiry*

---

> *The visual language does not illustrate the thesis. It is built from the same territory, in different materials.*

---

## What this process is

This document describes the method by which a visual language for queering contemplation is assembled across the 56-week curriculum — and from which an original series of artworks is made.

The process has two distinct phases that work in continuous relation:

**Phase one: looking and noticing.** Each week, two artists are studied — one historical, one contemporary — whose practice occupies territory adjacent to queering contemplation. This is not art historical survey. It is close looking with a particular quality of attention: what resonates? What in this body of work speaks to the same ground the thesis is investigating, even if the artist never named it that way?

**Phase two: codifying and making.** What resonates is named, recorded, and added to a growing lexicon of visual elements. Over time, this lexicon becomes the material from which original work is made — not by copying or quoting, but by having absorbed a language, the way a writer absorbs the rhythms of the writers they love and then writes in their own voice.

The result is a series of artworks that is genuinely mine, and genuinely in conversation with those who came before.

---

## The relationship to the artists studied

The artists in the curriculum are not sources to be cited, imitated, or illustrated. They are predecessors in a shared territory — makers who have worked in the vicinity of queering contemplation, by whatever name they called it. I am in relation to them the way any maker is in relation to those who made before them: with respect, with attention, with genuine curiosity about what they found, and with the intention of extending the conversation rather than repeating it.

This is how visual traditions transmit. A painter who spent years looking at Morandi does not make work that looks like Morandi; they make work that has absorbed what Morandi knew about fidelity to the unremarkable. A sculptor who sat long with Petah Coyne does not replicate Coyne's forms; they understand something about the body hovering between states that Coyne made visible, and that understanding enters their own work in ways that are entirely their own.

The visual language being built here is mine. It is built from what resonated across 112 artist encounters — a colour that held something about grief, a brushstroke that enacted non-dual awareness, a compositional structure that embodied lo cotidiano, a light source that felt like the window of tolerance made visible. These elements are not borrowed. They are learned from, and then spoken in a new sentence.

## What gets noticed and recorded

When studying each week's artist pair, attention is given to any visual element that resonates — that carries something of the territory of queering contemplation. There is no hierarchy of what counts. Any of the following may be significant:

**Materiality** — the specific physical qualities of the materials used and what those qualities do: the way wax holds and releases light, the way charcoal discharges and smudges, the weight of linen, the granularity of a particular pigment, what happens when ink meets wet paper

**Colour** — not colour theory in the abstract, but specific colour relationships, temperatures, saturations, and what they hold: the near-disappearance of Martin's whites, the specific grey of Brooks's palette, Delaney's luminosity, Bellini's blue

**Mark and surface** — the quality of a mark, a brushstroke, a line: whether it hesitates, whether it presses, whether it accumulates or subtracts, whether it shows the body that made it or conceals it

**Composition** — how the work organises space: what is given the centre, what is held at the edge, what is allowed to overflow the frame, how the eye is asked to move or to rest

**Subject** — not what the work is "about" in a thematic sense, but what bodies, objects, relationships, or moments are treated as worthy of sustained attention

**Light** — where light comes from, what it falls on, how it moves, whether it reveals or conceals, whether it is literal or felt

**Scale and gesture** — what the size of a work asks of the body encountering it, what a gesture at one scale means differently at another, how the relationship between maker's body and work's body is inscribed in the surface

**Process logic** — what the method enacts: duration, repetition, accumulation, subtraction, chance, material resistance, collaboration, seriality

**Structural moves** — operations the work performs that could be translated across media: holding rupture without resolving it, attending to the same thing until it becomes infinite, making absence present, letting process be visible in the product

## How the lexicon accumulates

Each week's artist encounter produces a small number of entries in a running lexicon — words, phrases, or named visual operations that are specific enough to be useful. Not "grief" but "colour held at near-disappearance." Not "the body" but "composition that gives the edge the weight the centre usually holds." Not "contemplation" but "mark that shows the duration of its making."

The lexicon grows across 56 weeks. Some entries appear once; others recur across multiple artists and constellations, accumulating resonance. Over time, patterns emerge: certain colour temperatures appear across multiple artists working in the territory of the dark night; certain compositional structures recur among artists working with lo cotidiano; certain mark qualities appear in artists whose practice is durational and somatic.

These patterns are not coincidences. They suggest something about what queering contemplation looks like when it is embodied in visual form — what the territory actually demands of the materials and the maker.

The accumulated lexicon is the visual language of queering contemplation as it has been discovered in this inquiry. It is the material from which the original series of works is made.

## How the original work is made

The artworks in the series are not made by consulting the lexicon consciously, the way one might look up a word in a dictionary. By the time making begins in earnest, the lexicon has been absorbed — it is inside the body, the eye, the hand. The making proceeds from that absorption.

What this means in practice: when a colour decision is made, it is informed by having spent time with the colour relationships of dozens of artists working in adjacent territory. When a compositional choice is made, it is informed by having noticed what composition does in the hands of makers who have held grief, or lo cotidiano, or the dark night as their material. When a mark is made, it carries something of what was learned from makers whose marks showed their bodies, or whose marks enacted fidelity, or whose marks held the tension between control and release.

The original work is mine because I am the one who looked, who noticed, who made the lexicon my own, and who then made from it. The artists who came before are present in it the way a teacher is present in a student's work: not as source material to be cited, but as the ones whose looking made my looking possible.

## The relationship between process and thesis

This process is not separate from the thesis inquiry. It is one of its two primary methods.

The thesis argues, in words, that the ordinary body can recognize itself as the primary site of the sacred through a non-linear art practice. The visual work demonstrates this claim in practice — not by illustrating it, but by being its enactment. The process of building the visual language is itself an instance of the hypothesis: a body (mine) engaging in sustained, non-linear practice across 56 weeks, in conversation with artists who came before, finding its own voice in that conversation.

The lexicon is evidence. The original series of works is evidence. Together they hold what the written thesis can argue but cannot fully contain: the felt knowledge of what queering contemplation looks like when it is made, not only when it is described.

## Repository structure for this process

The visual language process is documented in two places in this repository:

`VISUAL-LANGUAGE-PROCESS.md` — this document, describing the method

`weeks/` — each weekly file contains an **Artist Influence Log** section documenting what was noticed and what resonated in that week's artist pair, and recording vocabulary additions to the growing lexicon

The lexicon itself accumulates across the weekly logs and will be synthesised in the Phase VIII synthesis files (weeks 50–56), where the visual language is named as evidence for the thesis findings.
