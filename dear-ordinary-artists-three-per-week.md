# Dear Ordinary: Artist Reference
## One Historical · One Contemporary · One Emerging · Per Week
### 56 Weeks · Eight Phases

*These entries are designed to sit inside each week's existing structure, replacing the current art practice invitation or accompanying it. Each entry holds the week's star lightly — artists in conversation with the territory, not illustrations of it.*

---

## Phase I · Weeks 1–7 · The Body's Wisdom

---

### Week 01 · Interoception — the brain's reading of its own internal state

**Historical: Remedios Varo** (1908–1963)
Varo's paintings show figures in acts of absorbed interior attention — navigating by instrument, distilling the world into essence, receiving transmissions from unknown sources. Her bodies are never passive; they are always reading something from within. The self in her work is a sensitive instrument tuned to its own signal.

**Contemporary: Petah Coyne** (b. 1953)
Coyne builds suspended sculptures from wax, silk, taxidermied animals, and organic matter that seem to be in the process of becoming something — hovering between states, between living and not living. Her work insists on the body's felt reality as the primary site of knowledge, the place where the visible and invisible meet.

**Emerging: Sedrick Chisom** (b. 1987)
Chisom's large-scale paintings — densely layered figures dissolving into industrial and cosmic environments — track sensation at the threshold of intelligibility. His work asks what the body knows before language arrives, rendering interoceptive experience as apocalyptic visual field. He has exhibited widely including at the Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago and has been represented by Pilar Corrias gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Make a continuous mark that traces what you sensed *inside* your body in the last ten minutes — not what you thought, not what you felt emotionally, but what you sensed: pressure, temperature, the weight of a hand on a knee, where breath moved. Let the mark be a readout, not an image.

---

### Week 02 · The nervous system as navigator (Polyvagal theory)

**Historical: Käthe Kollwitz** (1867–1945)
Kollwitz worked in charcoal, etching, and lithography, making work about bodies under duress — the grieving mother, the starving child, the worker whose survival is communal or not at all. Her figures are never still; they curl, reach, collapse, hold each other. She understood that nervous systems in contact with each other are how survival happens.

**Contemporary: Cannupa Hanska Luger** (b. 1979)
Luger makes large-scale performance and object works that address collective body states — the body in protest, in ceremony, in the face of extractive violence. His Mirror Shield Project gave Standing Rock protesters reflective shields so the militarized police could see themselves in the bodies they were threatening. The work literalizes co-regulation as political act.

**Emerging: Chloë Bass** (b. 1984)
Bass's practice — spanning performance, text, and public installation — investigates the social structures that regulate nervous system states: care economies, proximity, the labor of attending to another person's body. Her project *The Book of Everyday Instruction* and ongoing *Wayfinding* series ask how collective regulation actually works in daily life. She is a Brooklyn-based artist and educator whose work has been exhibited at venues including the Brooklyn Museum.

**Practice invitation:** Before making anything, spend five minutes in a posture your body finds genuinely restful — not performed relaxation, actual ease. Notice what shifts. Make something from that state, however briefly it lasts. What does ventral vagal look like as a mark?

---

### Week 03 · Window of tolerance

**Historical: Agnes Martin** (1912–2004)
Martin's grids and horizontal washes — faint pencil on large fields, color barely distinguishable from white — enact the window of tolerance as visual experience. To stand before her work is to feel something settle in the nervous system. She described her paintings as about "innocence of mind": not naivety, but a return to a state before reactivity takes over.

**Contemporary: Roni Horn** (b. 1955)
Horn's glass cylinder sculptures look solid but move like liquid — they hold a state that appears stable but is always in process. Her *You Are the Weather* series photographs a single woman's face in geothermal pools across 100 images, ranging the full spectrum of the nervous system's readable states within one body.

**Emerging: Allison Gildersleeve** (b. 1978)
Gildersleeve's paintings occupy the territory of just-perceptible difference — subtle tonal shifts, nearly-but-not-quite repetition, states that hold without resolving. Her work creates a visual analog for the regulated nervous system: present, grounded, available. She has exhibited at Freight+Volume gallery and other venues in New York and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make something from within your actual window right now — not from where you wish you were. If the window is currently narrow, let that be the material. A contracted mark. A small form. Constraint as honesty.

---

### Week 04 · Trauma metabolized

**Historical: Hannah Wilke** (1940–1993)
Wilke made her body the medium — casting her own flesh in chewing gum, photographing herself across decades from seductive to ravaged, documenting her cancer treatment with unflinching self-portraiture. Her work insists that the body's full story be told by the body itself, not translated into something more comfortable for the viewer to receive.

**Contemporary: Kara Walker** (b. 1969)
Walker's monumental silhouettes make unmetabolized histories of racial violence visible in the room — cut black paper against white walls, the body of history standing in the gallery. Her work does what the nervous system does with unprocessed trauma: stores it in image, returns it as sensation in the viewer's body.

**Emerging: Tau Lewis** (b. 1993)
Lewis, a Toronto-based artist of Jamaican and Trinidadian heritage, makes hand-sewn sculptural figures from found and reclaimed materials — leather, fabric, hair — that hold the body's history of rupture, migration, and assembly. Her work understands trauma metabolized as literally stitching: the broken material made whole through sustained manual attention. She has exhibited at MoMA PS1, the Museum of Arts and Design in New York, and was included in the 2022 Whitney Biennial.

**Practice invitation:** Make something that lets a weight you are carrying be present in the work — not explained, not resolved, not made beautiful. Just present. The weight is allowed to stay.

---

### Week 05 · The difficult side of practice

**Historical: Yayoi Kusama** (b. 1929)
Kusama has worked through her own psychiatric disturbance for over seven decades, making obsessive accumulation — dots, nets, infinity rooms — the method by which she metabolizes overwhelming inner states. She has lived voluntarily in a psychiatric institution since 1977 and goes to her studio every day. Her practice is not in spite of her disturbance; it is made *from* it, with rigorous discipline.

**Contemporary: Sonya Clark** (b. 1967)
Clark's *Unraveling* project — she unraveled a Confederate flag one thread at a time in public spaces, inviting passersby to participate — asks what it costs to undo a violent inheritance, thread by thread. The difficulty of the process is the work's argument.

**Emerging: Sable Elyse Smith** (b. 1986)
Smith's paintings, sculptures, and video works hold the violence of the carceral system — her father was incarcerated for much of her childhood — as the difficult territory from which practice must emerge. Her work refuses to aestheticize or resolve the difficulty, insisting on its ongoing, embodied cost. She has exhibited widely including at the ICA Philadelphia and the 2019 Whitney Biennial, and is represented by JTT gallery and Carlos/Ishikawa.

**Practice invitation:** Make something in the presence of difficulty rather than in its absence. Stay with a process that is genuinely hard for you — physically demanding, emotionally activating, uncomfortably slow — for the duration of one work. Notice where you want to stop. Notice what the difficulty is asking you to face.

---

### Week 06 · Contemplative neuroscience

**Historical: Hilma af Klint** (1862–1944)
Af Klint made her *Paintings for the Temple* during states of sustained practice she understood as mediated — receiving what could not be received through ordinary attention. She kept the work from public view for twenty years after her death, believing the world was not yet structurally ready for what sustained practice can produce. She was right.

**Contemporary: Sung Tieu** (b. 1987)
Tieu's installations draw on her experience of displacement and bureaucratic violence, making sensory environments — sound, light, architecture — that alter the viewer's neurological state as a form of argument. Her practice understands that space reorganizes the nervous system, and that this is a political as much as an aesthetic fact.

**Emerging: Precious Okoyomon** (b. 1993)
Okoyomon's installations combine living plants, soil, text, and scent to create immersive environments that engage the viewer's full sensory nervous system. Their work at the 2022 Documenta fifteen — a sprawling garden installation titled *To See the Earth Before the End of the World* — asked what sustained contemplative attention to the natural world produces in the body and the politics. They are a poet and visual artist based in New York.

**Practice invitation:** Make something with the explicit intention of sustained, absorbed attention — not to produce a good piece, but to inhabit the making as completely as possible for as long as possible. Afterward, write three sentences about what changed in the body during the making.

---

### Week 07 · The body as primary text (Phase I synthesis)

**Historical: Ana Mendieta** (1948–1985)
Mendieta's *Silueta* series — body-shaped voids pressed, burned, and traced into earth across Cuba and Iowa — is the closest visual practice to this curriculum's central claim. The body is not separate from the earth or from the sacred; it is already made of those things. Her absence-presences in soil and fire hold loss, belonging, and the sacred as one event.

**Contemporary: Nicholas Galanin** (b. 1979)
Galanin's practice — spanning carving, video, performance, and installation — refuses the separation of Indigenous body, land, and knowledge. His work holds the body as the location where ancient and living wisdom resides, not as a site of colonial inventory or spiritual abstraction.

**Emerging: Meriem Bennani** (b. 1988)
Bennani's video and installation works — often set in near-future or speculative worlds — hold the body as a text being read and misread by systems of power: surveillance, immigration, algorithmic culture. Her work asks what the body knows about itself that systems cannot capture. She is a Moroccan-American artist based in New York who has exhibited at the Museum of Modern Art, the New Museum, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Go outside. Leave a trace of your body in or on the earth — an impression in soft ground, a form from what you find, a temporary mark. Make it without photographing it. Return the next day. Make a record of what remains and what doesn't. The impermanence is not the loss — it is the whole statement.

## Phase II · Weeks 8–14 · The Hearthstone

---

### Week 08 · Self-compassion as ground (North Star)

**Historical: Mary Cassatt** (1844–1926)
Cassatt's intimate paintings of women and children bathing, reading, tending one another — scenes of the domestic and the relational — treat ordinary self-care as worthy of the same formal seriousness as any historical subject. Her work refuses the hierarchy that places ambition above care, or the self above the body that holds it.

**Contemporary: Tourmaline** (b. 1983)
Tourmaline's films, archives, and organizing make space for Black trans life as something to be cherished, not only survived. Her work holds self-compassion as a political act — the refusal to treat your own life as expendable is itself a form of resistance, and a form of theology.

**Emerging: Aria Dean** (b. 1993)
Dean's practice — spanning sculpture, video, writing, and curatorial work — holds the Black body's relationship to self-compassion within a theoretical framework that takes Blackness seriously as aesthetic and political ground. Her essay writing and sculptural practice refuse the demand that Black artists perform suffering or triumph, insisting on the ordinary as sufficient. She is an artist, writer, and curator who has exhibited at Greene Naftali Gallery in New York and written for *Artforum* and *e-flux*.

**Practice invitation:** Write a letter to yourself — not the self you wish you were, but the self you actually are this week. Write it the way you would write to a good friend who was carrying exactly what you are carrying. Then make something in response to the letter.

---

### Week 09 · Rupture

**Historical: Lee Krasner** (1908–1984)
Krasner's large collages — made by cutting up her own previous canvases and reassembling the pieces — enact rupture as material. She literally tore her own work apart. The joins in the surface are the history; the ruptures are not hidden but held as part of the composition. Her practice understands that the broken thing is still a thing.

**Contemporary: Zanele Muholi** (b. 1972)
Muholi's *Faces and Phases* series — over 500 portraits of Black lesbian and trans South Africans — documents lives subject to rupture by systemic violence. Their *Somnyama Ngonyama* self-portrait series transforms that rupture into sovereignty: the photographed body takes up the full frame without apology.

**Emerging: Didier William** (b. 1983)
William's carved and painted panels — layered surfaces of eyes, bodies, and symbols drawn from Haitian cosmology, queer identity, and art historical citation — hold rupture as generative. His work understands the fractured surface as a record of survival: what breaks you is also, finally, what you are made of. He is a Haitian-born, Philadelphia-based artist who has exhibited at the Museum of Fine Arts Boston, the Studio Museum in Harlem, and was a finalist for the Colby Museum's Lunder Prize.

**Practice invitation:** Make something. Then rupture it — cut, tear, crumple, overpaint, burn an edge. Don't do this to destroy the work; do it to tell the truth about the work's life. Decide whether to leave it ruptured or attempt repair. Either choice is the work. There is no correct answer.

---

### Week 10 · Repair

**Historical: Cecilia Vicuña** (b. 1948)
Vicuña's quipu installations — fragile fiber works suspended in gallery space, assembled from found and discarded materials — hold grief and repair together as one gesture. Each knot in a quipu is a record; each join is a memory. Her work understands repair not as erasing the break but as honoring it with sustained attention.

**Contemporary: Theaster Gates** (b. 1973)
Gates salvages materials from destroyed Black institutions — floors from demolished schools, fire hoses from Civil Rights protests, library collections from shuttered branches — and transforms civic rupture into art and architecture. His Stony Island Arts Bank in Chicago is repair at urban scale, made from what was almost lost.

**Emerging: Simone Leigh** (b. 1967)
Leigh's large-scale bronze sculptures and ceramic works — the female Black body monumentalized, given the scale of civic architecture — hold repair as political claim: the refusal to accept that the wound is permanent, the insistence on building new structures of care. She represented the United States at the 2022 Venice Biennale, winning the Golden Lion, the Biennale's top prize. Though now widely recognized, her decades of community-focused practice exemplifies the sustained repair work this curriculum describes.

**Practice invitation:** Find something broken in your environment — ordinary, not precious. Repair it using a method that does not hide the repair: visible stitching, an obvious patch, a join that announces itself. Let the history of the break be held in the surface rather than smoothed over.

---

### Week 11 · The human in you is the human in me

**Historical: Käthe Kollwitz** (1867–1945)
Kollwitz's self-portraits, made across four decades, track the aging of a face that has held grief, poverty, war, and the deaths of those most loved. They are not about individualism; they are about what a single human face carries on behalf of all faces. To look at a late Kollwitz self-portrait is to feel less alone in your own.

**Contemporary: Titus Kaphar** (b. 1976)
Kaphar's paintings — figures emerging from or being swallowed by obliterated canvas, monuments being rewrapped in white cloth — ask who gets included in the category of "human" in Western art's inherited archive, and at what cost to those left out of the frame.

**Emerging: Toyin Ojih Odutola** (b. 1985)
Ojih Odutola's drawings — built up from dense, layered ballpoint pen marks into luminous skin and complex sociological worlds — insist on the full interiority of the Black figures she depicts. Her work holds "the human in you is the human in me" as a visual argument: the figures she draws are not archetypes but irreducible persons. She has exhibited at the Whitney Museum of American Art, the Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago, and Jack Shainman Gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Make a portrait — of anyone, any medium. The only constraint: the person you are making cannot become an object while you make them. Let them remain a subject throughout. Notice what this demands of your attention.

---

### Week 12 · Recognition

**Historical: Frida Kahlo** (1907–1954)
Kahlo's double self-portraits — *The Two Fridas*, *Diego and I* — are paintings of recognition and its failure: the self seeing itself, the self seen and unseen by the beloved. Her work holds recognition as simultaneously the deepest form of pain and the deepest form of belonging. The two cannot be separated in her paintings.

**Contemporary: Ellen Gallagher** (b. 1965)
Gallagher's large-scale works layer found imagery from Black publications with her own marks, exploring what it means to be recognized — to find your face in the archive, to see your image in print, to be named by a medium that has historically refused to name you.

**Emerging: Salman Toor** (b. 1983)
Toor's intimate paintings of queer South Asian men — in apartments, bars, on phones, holding each other — create a world in which recognition between figures who have historically been invisible to mainstream representation is the central act. The paintings hold the relief of being seen, and the tenderness of seeing, as the same gesture. He is a Pakistani-American artist based in New York who has exhibited at the Whitney Museum of American Art and is represented by Luhring Augustine gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Make a two-part work: the first holding the self that has not yet been recognized; the second holding what full recognition — without condition — would feel like in the body. The distance between the two is the whole statement.

---

### Week 13 · The long arc of rupture and repair

**Historical: Louise Bourgeois** (1911–2010)
Bourgeois's spider sculptures — enormous, patient, both protecting and devouring — are about the long arc of rupture and repair across a lifetime. Her enclosed *Cells*, containing objects from her own past, are literally the architecture of repair: the things that wounded you, housed carefully, tended, made into a form you can live near.

**Contemporary: Sonia Gomes** (b. 1948)
Gomes's sculptures, made from knotted, bound, and tangled fabric emerging from an Afro-Brazilian spiritual tradition, understand the body as always in process of being assembled and disassembled. Her work holds the long arc of repair as something that extends beyond a single lifetime.

**Emerging: Sky Hopinka** (b. 1984)
Hopinka's films — made in Indigenous languages, moving between documentary and lyric, between the present and the ancestral — hold the long arc of rupture and repair across generations and landscapes. His work understands that repair is not a single act but a sustained practice of return. He is a Ho-Chunk Nation member and Pechanga Band of Luiseño Indians descendant whose films have screened at Sundance, the Berlinale, and major museums internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work that takes the full week to complete by returning to it daily — one layer, one mark, one element each day. On day one, you cannot know what day seven will hold. The practice is the daily return.

---

### Week 14 · The Hearthstone (Phase II synthesis)

**Historical: Faith Ringgold** (b. 1930)
Ringgold's story quilts — painting, fabric, and text combined — hold the hearthstone's entire territory: the domestic as political, the ordinary as archive, the self-compassionate act of telling your own story in materials that refuse the fine art hierarchy. *Tar Beach* holds a child's longing, a family's survival, and a city's racism in the same surface.

**Contemporary: Firelei Báez** (b. 1981)
Báez's large-scale paintings — figures emerging from historical maps and archival documents — ask what it would mean to repair the colonial archive by insisting on the presence of those it erased. Her work is about claiming the hearthstone that a particular history said you were not allowed to occupy.

**Emerging: Njideka Akunyili Crosby** (b. 1983)
*Noted here for her hearthstone practice.* Akunyili Crosby's large paintings of domestic interiors — layered with photo transfers of Nigerian popular culture and lived experience — make the domestic space a site of complex cultural identity: the hearthstone as the place where what you carry from one world meets what you are building in another. Her work has been exhibited at the Whitney Museum, the National Portrait Gallery in London, and she was awarded a MacArthur Fellowship in 2017.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work about the place where you feel most yourself — the actual place, not the one you think you should feel most yourself. Let it be as ordinary as it actually is. Use the materials of that place if you can.

## Phase III · Weeks 15–21 · The Mystical Thread

---

### Week 15 · Non-dual awareness

**Historical: Utagawa Hiroshige** (1797–1858)
Hiroshige's woodblock prints — rain falling across a bridge, travelers in a landscape that dwarfs them, fog swallowing a mountain — place the human figure inside a world it cannot be separated from. The traveler is not in the rain; the traveler is rain, briefly walking. Subject and world are one process.

**Contemporary: Rirkrit Tiravanija** (b. 1961)
Tiravanija cooks and serves food in gallery spaces, turning exhibitions into social environments where the artwork only exists in the encounter. The boundary between art and life, artist and viewer, subject and object dissolves. There is no separate art object to preserve.

**Emerging: Pita Arreola** (b. 1992)
Arreola's paintings hold figures and environments in states of permeability — the body bleeding into the landscape, the interior bleeding into the exterior — enacting non-dual awareness as painterly fact. Her work, emerging from a Chicana feminist practice, refuses the Western tradition's insistence on the bounded, separate self. She is a Los Angeles-based artist who has exhibited at the Los Angeles County Museum of Art and Proyectos Monclova in Mexico City.

**Practice invitation:** Make something without stopping to evaluate it. Set a timer for forty minutes and do not step back, do not assess, do not correct. Stay in contact with the material without interruption. Notice what happens to the sense of a separate self doing the making.

---

### Week 16 · Via negativa

**Historical: Mark Rothko** (1903–1970)
Rothko's late paintings — especially the Rothko Chapel — create conditions for encounter with the numinous through subtraction: large fields of color so close in value they nearly disappear into each other, forms that resist being named. He understood his paintings as thresholds, not objects. He wept when told they made people weep.

**Contemporary: Jennie C. Jones** (b. 1968)
Jones works at the intersection of visual art, music, and silence — making monochromes embedded with acoustic panels, paintings that absorb sound, works that ask what cannot be heard as a form of knowledge. Her practice is the via negativa experienced through the senses before it reaches the mind.

**Emerging: Dindga McCannon** (b. 1947)
*Though not emerging chronologically, McCannon's sustained practice of reduction and stripping-back deserves placement here.* Her textile works hold the via negativa as a feminist quilting tradition: making visible what has been systematically subtracted from the record. She is a founding member of the Black women's art collective Where We At (1971) and has exhibited at the Brooklyn Museum and Studio Museum in Harlem. Her practice of radical simplicity across six decades speaks directly to this week's territory.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work by removing rather than adding. Begin with something present — a full surface, a loaded mark — and work by taking away. The work is finished not when it looks complete but when what remains is only what cannot be removed.

---

### Week 17 · Kenosis: self-emptying

**Historical: Simone Martini** (c. 1284–1344)
Martini's *Annunciation* altarpiece captures the moment of kenosis — the divine emptying into the human, and the human receiving with a body that simultaneously recoils and consents. Mary's posture is not serene acceptance; it is the full complexity of a being encountering something that will cost her everything and give her everything.

**Contemporary: Pope.L** (1955–2023)
Pope.L's crawling performances — dragging his body across city streets, up flagpoles, through impossible public spaces — are acts of chosen vulnerability that refuse to look like strength while insisting on its presence. His practice asks: what does power look like when it empties itself of the performance of power?

**Emerging: Cameron Rowland** (b. 1988)
Rowland's installations hold the logic of kenosis at a structural level — works that refuse to accumulate value, objects that insist on diminishment rather than increase. Their practice examines the economic and legal structures underlying racial capitalism, using objects that cannot be sold separately from the conditions of their making. They have exhibited at Artists Space in New York, the 2017 Whitney Biennial, and the ICA London.

**Practice invitation:** Make something using only your non-dominant hand. Do not correct it with your dominant hand. Let incompetence be the practice. What emerges from the hand that doesn't know what it's doing is a record of a kind of trust you cannot access any other way.

---

### Week 18 · Discernment

**Historical: Georgia O'Keeffe** (1887–1986)
O'Keeffe's decades-long practice of discernment — knowing what to paint, when to leave New York, how to say no to interpretations that diminished her work — is as much the practice as the paintings themselves. Her bleached skulls and enormous flowers are acts of sustained attending to what the self actually finds sacred, regardless of what the market wants.

**Contemporary: Kiki Smith** (b. 1954)
Smith's practice holds discernment at the cellular and mythological levels simultaneously — she makes work from what her body and her Catholic-inflected mythology tell her matters, without seeking external permission for the combination. Her work discerns what is life-giving by following what her hands say is true.

**Emerging: Genevieve Gaignard** (b. 1981)
Gaignard's photography and installation work — in which she stages herself as various personas inhabiting the aesthetics of American domesticity — holds discernment as the practice of distinguishing between the self that is expected and the self that is true. A biracial woman whose work examines race, class, and performance, she asks what it means to discern your own identity within a culture that constantly assigns you one. She has exhibited at the Hammer Museum in Los Angeles, the ICA Boston, and is represented by Françoise Ghebaly gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Before making anything, write two lists: what has been life-giving in your practice this year, and what has been death-dealing — what has narrowed you, drained you, asked you to be less than you are. Make something this week that comes only from the life-giving column.

---

### Week 19 · Cross-traditional encounter

**Historical: Rabindranath Tagore** (1861–1941)
Tagore — poet, musician, visual artist — made his abstract and calligraphic drawings in his seventies as a practice drawing from Bengali, Japanese, and European modernist visual languages simultaneously. He understood cross-traditional encounter not as synthesis but as a third thing belonging fully to none of its sources.

**Contemporary: Shahzia Sikander** (b. 1969)
Sikander's practice draws from Indo-Persian miniature painting, Western abstraction, Hindu mythology, Islamic geometric tradition, and feminist theory — not by combining them decoratively but by finding where they genuinely speak to each other about shared human territory.

**Emerging: Tuan Andrew Nguyen** (b. 1976)
Nguyen's film and sculpture practice holds cross-traditional encounter at the level of historical trauma and healing — his work moves between Vietnamese oral tradition, Buddhist cosmology, and Western documentary form to make visible what no single tradition can hold alone. He is a founding member of the art collective The Propeller Group and has exhibited at the 2019 Venice Biennale, Sundance, and the Museum of Modern Art.

**Practice invitation:** Spend genuine time with a contemplative art practice from a tradition not your own — not as appropriation but as encounter. Then make something in response: not an imitation, but a reply. What did the encounter ask of your own practice?

---

### Week 20 · The mystery held openly

**Historical: Paul Klee** (1879–1940)
Klee's notebooks and paintings hold mystery as a method rather than a problem. His *Pedagogical Sketchbook* and *Thinking Eye* are records of a mind that refuses to resolve what does not need resolving — that takes pleasure in the open question, the unfinished form, the mark that knows more than the maker does.

**Contemporary: Teresita Fernández** (b. 1968)
Fernández makes works that use light, reflection, and material in ways that refuse to be fully seen — you can never resolve her mirrored coal installations into a single image. The mystery is not a decorative quality but a structural one, built into how the work physically behaves.

**Emerging: Shikeith** (b. 1989)
Shikeith's photography and video works hold the Black male body in states of vulnerability, dream, and intimacy that refuse to be fully named — the figures he photographs are in the territory of the mystery, not the territory of legibility. His practice asks what remains when the demand for explanation is removed. He is a Philadelphia-based artist who has exhibited at the Pérez Art Museum Miami, the Studio Museum in Harlem, and the ICA Philadelphia.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work you are not allowed to title, name, or explain to anyone who asks. Make it anyway, as fully and seriously as you can. Live with the unnamed work for one week. Notice what naming would foreclose.

---

### Week 21 · The Mystical Thread (Phase III synthesis)

**Historical: El Greco** (1541–1614)
El Greco's elongated figures, electric skies, gold that is not quite gold — his entire visual language enacts a body in the presence of something beyond itself. His *View of Toledo* shows a landscape as if lit from within by its own drama. He understood that the via negativa, kenosis, non-dual awareness, and discernment are one continuous experience, only made separate by language.

**Contemporary: Kara Walker** (b. 1969)
Walker's silhouettes — black on white, the absolute clarity of shadow — hold the mystical and the political in the same cut line. Her work understands that the sacred cannot be separated from history's violence; that non-dual awareness includes the horror and the holiness in the same field.

**Emerging: Firelei Báez** (b. 1981)
*Returning for the synthesis.* Báez's Phase III work holds the mystical thread as the insistence that the body of the historically erased is also the body in mystical encounter. Her recent large-scale installations — figures merging with historical documents, cartographic records of colonial violence — understand that the via negativa, for the colonized subject, is not a choice but the condition of survival made luminous.

**Practice invitation:** Make a diptych: one work from as much control as you can bring, one from as much surrender as you can allow. Lay them side by side. The gap between them is the mystical thread.

## Phase IV · Weeks 22–28 · The Engaged Path

---

### Week 22 · Liberation theology — God found among those who suffer

**Historical: José Clemente Orozco** (1883–1949)
Orozco's murals — unlike Rivera's programmatic optimism — hold the suffering and complicity of history without resolution. His *Epic of American Civilization* at Dartmouth shows the violence of colonialism and the church in the same frame as their supposed redemption. He refused to let the sacred float above the wound.

**Contemporary: Tiona Nekkia McClodden** (b. 1981)
McClodden's practice — video, sound, sculpture, and archival work — centers Black queer and trans experience as the location where the sacred is most honestly encountered. Her work understands liberation theology not as comfort but as the demand to look at what has actually happened to actual bodies.

**Emerging: Alia Farid** (b. 1985)
Farid's films and installations hold liberation theology from the perspective of communities living under occupation and economic dispossession in Kuwait, Puerto Rico, and across the Global South. Her work asks where God is found in the ordinary lives of those whom extraction has made expendable. She has exhibited at the 2022 Documenta fifteen, the Sharjah Biennial, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work that begins from a specific injustice — not abstract, but one you can name, with a body attached to it. Let the injustice be the ground from which the work grows. You don't need to make it legible to a viewer. You need to make it honestly.

---

### Week 23 · Interbeing — the radical interdependence of all phenomena

**Historical: Paul Cézanne** (1839–1906)
Cézanne's late paintings dissolve the boundary between figure and ground, solid and atmospheric, looking and being looked at. His *Mont Sainte-Victoire* series shows the same mountain across decades as the light changes, as his own perception deepens — the mountain becoming not an object but a relationship between painter and place.

**Contemporary: Neri Oxman** (1976–2024)
Oxman's practice — biologically grown architecture, materials made from living organisms, structures where the boundary between designed and grown is dissolved — enacted interbeing as a material principle. Her Silk Pavilion, built by 6,500 silkworms, is a building that is also an animal act.

**Emerging: Dala Nasser** (b. 1992)
Nasser's paintings hold ecology, geology, and the human figure in states of mutual permeability — the body as sediment, the landscape as skin. Her work, emerging from a Lebanese context of ecological and political crisis, understands interbeing not as spiritual concept but as material fact of survival. She has exhibited at the Sharjah Art Foundation and internationally, and is represented by Galerie Imane Farès in Paris.

**Practice invitation:** Make something in response to something you did not make — a natural system, a weather event, another person's action, a sound. Let the work be a reply, not a depiction.

---

### Week 24 · Lo cotidiano — the kitchen table is a theological location

**Historical: Jean-Baptiste-Siméon Chardin** (1699–1779)
Chardin's still lifes — copper pots, bread, a single glass of water, a child blowing a soap bubble — make the domestic luminous without sentimentalizing it. His paint handles the ordinary with the same gravity it would bring to a Madonna. The kitchen is already sacred; you don't need to transform it.

**Contemporary: Carmen Herrera** (1915–2022)
Herrera made her paintings — sharp, geometric, uncompromising — in her apartment kitchen while her husband worked. For decades she sold nothing. She received her first major institutional recognition at 89. Her practice is the most radical lo cotidiano: seventy years of serious, uncompromising work in the ordinary space of daily life, before anyone arrived to witness it.

**Emerging: Tomashi Jackson** (b. 1980)
Jackson's paintings layer domestic objects, textile patterns, archival footage, and juridical language to make visible the theological weight of everyday Black life in America. Her work holds lo cotidiano as a site where the legal, the domestic, the sacred, and the political cannot be separated. She has exhibited at the Studio Museum in Harlem, the 2021 Whitney Biennial, and the ICA Boston.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work from the materials of your ordinary day — not your art supplies, but what is already present where you live and eat and rest. Let the materials carry the theology without being elevated into art.

---

### Week 25 · The erotic as knowledge and resistance

**Historical: Titian** (c. 1488–1576)
Titian's late mythological paintings — *Diana and Actaeon*, *Venus with a Mirror* — hold the erotic not as decoration but as a site of profound knowledge about power, sovereignty, and what it means to be seen without consent. His final paintings are about the dangerous territory of the erotic, rendered without flinching.

**Contemporary: Mickalene Thomas** (b. 1971)
Thomas's large-scale paintings and photographs of Black women — decadent, rhinestone-encrusted, claiming the full space of the canvas and the gaze — reclaim the erotic as a source of Black women's power and self-knowledge. Her work refuses the desexualization that racism and white feminism have jointly imposed on Black women's bodies.

**Emerging: Billie Zangewa** (b. 1973)
Zangewa's hand-stitched silk tapestries — intimate domestic scenes of a Black woman's daily life, desire, and self-possession — hold the erotic in Audre Lorde's sense: the life force that insists on its own fullness. Her work is a visual elaboration of what self-knowledge through the erotic looks like in the body of a Black woman navigating Johannesburg. She has exhibited at Lehmann Maupin gallery, the Goodman Gallery, and the 2022 Venice Biennale.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work from your deepest longing — not necessarily sexual, but the erotic in Lorde's sense: the felt knowledge of what you most want to be, make, know, and give. Let the material carry the charge. Not a work *about* desire — a work *from* it.

---

### Week 26 · Engaged practice — inner and outer transformation are not separate

**Historical: Francisco Goya** (1746–1828)
Goya's *Black Paintings* — painted directly onto the walls of his house, for no audience but himself — are among the most honest records of what inner transformation looks like when it holds the violence of the world without flinching and without resolution. His *Saturn Devouring His Son* is not despair; it is unflinching witness. The practice made what no audience had commissioned.

**Contemporary: Theaster Gates** (b. 1973)
Gates's practice moves between inner transformation and structural change without privileging either. His salvaging of materials from destroyed Black institutions, and his transformation of those materials into functioning community spaces, understands that contemplation without action is incomplete, and action without contemplation is unsustainable.

**Emerging: Sreshta Rit Premnath** (b. 1979)
Premnath's practice holds engaged practice as a question about the political economy of artistic labor: what does it mean to make work from within the contradictions of a system you are simultaneously critiquing? His paintings, sculptures, and editorial work with the journal *Shifter* (which he co-founded) refuse the separation of inner and outer transformation. He has exhibited at the Museum of Modern Art, the Kunsthalle Basel, and is represented by Essex Street gallery in New York.

**Practice invitation:** Make something in direct response to something happening in the world right now — not as protest art, not as illustration of a position, but as your body's honest response to outer reality. What does your body hold about what is happening?

---

### Week 27 · No specialness required

**Historical: Giorgio Morandi** (1890–1964)
Morandi spent decades painting the same small collection of bottles, jars, and boxes on his studio table. The same objects, slightly rearranged, in shifting light, for forty years. His practice is the most radical refusal of specialness: nothing new is required; the same ordinary things, attended to with complete fidelity, are infinite.

**Contemporary: Martine Syms** (b. 1988)
Syms's video and installation work treats Black everyday life — vernacular speech, casual gesture, domestic ritual, the aesthetics of the ordinary — as archive, as theory, and as art. Her practice insists that no specialness is required; the ordinary is already doing the work.

**Emerging: Zoe Williams** (b. 1991)
Williams's paintings of interiors, kitchenware, fabric, and domestic objects hold the ordinary with the same loving fidelity that Morandi brought to his bottles — but from a Black British domestic perspective, insisting that this specific ordinariness is as worthy of sustained attention as any canonical subject. She is a London-based painter who has exhibited at Tate Modern and internationally, and is represented by Pilar Corrias gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Choose the most unremarkable object in your immediate environment — not the beautiful one, the difficult one, or the meaningful one; the most ordinary. Make five works from it across the week. The practice is fidelity to the unremarkable.

---

### Week 28 · The Engaged Path (Phase IV synthesis)

**Historical: Diego Rivera** (1886–1957)
Rivera's Detroit Industry Murals hold workers' bodies, industrial processes, scientific research, and the cycles of the earth in the same 27-panel work. The engaged path is not separate from beauty, from mysticism, from the daily, or from the body; Rivera's murals make that argument at architectural scale without apology.

**Contemporary: Hank Willis Thomas** (b. 1976)
Thomas's practice moves between contemplative image-making and direct civic engagement without marking a boundary between them. His *Truth Booth* — an inflatable lie-detector-shaped installation brought to conflict zones around the world — invited ordinary people to speak their truth into a structure made from absurdity and care.

**Emerging: Lauren Halsey** (b. 1987)
Halsey's architecturally scaled works — carved hieroglyph columns, community structures, dense archival assemblages of South Central Los Angeles culture — hold the engaged path's synthesis: inner devotion and outer transformation as one continuous act. Her practice is rooted in her community, her studio is in her neighborhood, and her work insists that those two facts are inseparable. She represented the United States at the 2024 Venice Biennale and has exhibited at the Metropolitan Museum of Art.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work that holds your interior life and your political life in the same frame — not as a diagram of their relationship, but as a single, integrated image. Where do they touch in your body? Make from that place of contact.

---

## Phase V · Weeks 29–35 · The Wide Waters

---

### Week 29 · Earth as theological partner

**Historical: John Constable** (1776–1837)
Constable's cloud studies — hundreds of oil sketches made outdoors across several years, labeled on the back with date, time, and weather conditions — are among Western art's most sustained encounters with the nonhuman world as a subject demanding the same seriousness as history or portraiture. He understood the sky as a living theological text that needed recording.

**Contemporary: Cannupa Hanska Luger** (b. 1979)
*Returning from Week 2 in new territory.* Luger's practice understands land not as property or background but as relative, as partner, as the entity with primary standing in any negotiation about its use. His work makes Indigenous ecological theology visible as political fact.

**Emerging: Asad Raza** (b. 1974)
Raza's installations hold earth as active participant — literally, in projects where living soil systems, plants, and atmospheric processes are the co-authors of the work. His project *Root Sequence. Mother Tongue* at the 2017 Whitney Biennial installed a living ecosystem of trees, soil, and organisms in the gallery, asking viewers to encounter the earth as theological partner rather than backdrop. He is a New York-based artist and curator who has exhibited internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Go outside and spend two hours with one small area — a square meter of ground, a single tree. Do not take your phone. Make marks in response to what you observe: not sketches of what it looks like, but responses to what it is *doing*. This is not nature study. This is theological encounter.

---

### Week 30 · Exile as contemplative location

**Historical: Paul Gauguin** (1848–1903)
Gauguin's exile to Tahiti was colonial in its logic, extractive in its practice. His work raises the question exile always raises: *whose exile, and at whose expense?* The contemplative location of the margin is not available to those who made others exile themselves to get there. His paintings hold beauty and violence in the same surface — and the tension cannot be resolved.

**Contemporary: Zanele Muholi** (b. 1972)
Muholi's practice operates from the margin of the margin — Black, queer, South African, feminist — and has transformed that location into a global archive of lives that the insider's world refuses to see. The exile knows things the insider cannot; Muholi's work makes that knowledge irrefutable.

**Emerging: Mounira Al Solh** (b. 1978)
Al Solh's drawing, embroidery, video, and performance practice has been shaped by displacement — from Lebanon, through Europe, navigating the experience of exile as a condition that produces particular forms of knowledge. Her series *I Strongly Believe in Our Right to Be Frivolous* gathers testimonies from Syrian refugees in Lebanon, refusing to reduce exile to suffering. She has exhibited at the 2015 Venice Biennale, the Sharjah Biennial, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make something in an unusual location — outside, in a stairwell, in a space that is not a studio. Let the non-institutional space do something to the making. Notice what changes when the system's jurisdiction is absent.

---

### Week 31 · The mystery held openly — negative capability

**Historical: J.M.W. Turner** (1775–1851)
Turner's late paintings dissolve the world into atmospheric events that the eye cannot resolve. He reputedly lashed himself to a ship's mast in a storm to paint from inside it. His practice is negative capability made luminous: remaining inside the overwhelming thing long enough to paint it, without grasping after resolution.

**Contemporary: Wolfgang Laib** (b. 1950)
Laib collects pollen from a single species at a time, on hands and knees in fields, over months — then pours it in vast glowing fields on gallery floors. No fixed meaning. No narrative. Just the overwhelming fact of that much attention paid to that much ordinary material. The mystery is held in the practice, not explained by the work.

**Emerging: Vivian Suter** (b. 1949)
Suter's paintings — made in her garden in Panajachel, Guatemala, left outside to be marked by rain, mud, dogs, and time — hold negative capability as the willingness to let what you cannot control become co-author of the work. Her vast, unstretched canvases refuse resolution into a single image. She has exhibited at Documenta 14, the Serpentine Gallery in London, and the Art Institute of Chicago.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work you are not allowed to explain — even to yourself. Begin without a concept. Make until something surprises you. Stop before you understand it. The unresolved is the whole point.

---

### Week 32 · Purpose as love expressed through suffering

**Historical: Emil Nolde** (1867–1956)
Nolde continued painting during the Nazi regime's prohibition of his work — making hundreds of small watercolors in secret he called his "Unpainted Pictures." Made for no audience, under genuine threat, from a practice he refused to abandon, they are records of what making looks like when it is stripped of every external purpose and retains only the internal one.

**Contemporary: Kara Walker** (b. 1969)
*Returning from Phase III.* Walker has said her work comes from a place of deep necessity — she makes what she makes because not making it would be a form of spiritual death. Purpose as love expressed, through the most difficult material, without the comfort of a guaranteed audience.

**Emerging: Ibrahim Mahama** (b. 1987)
Mahama's monumental installations — vast networks of hand-stitched jute sacks, the worn material of Ghanaian trade and labor, wrapped around buildings, suspended in galleries — hold purpose as the accumulated weight of many hands. Each stitch was made by workers he paid fairly in Kumasi; the work is the love of that labor expressed at architectural scale. He has exhibited at Documenta 14, the 2015 and 2019 Venice Biennales, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work about what you are most here to do — not your career, not your role, but the thing that feels like the truest expression of love available to you in this lifetime. It might be small and ordinary. Make it. Put it where you will see it daily.

---

### Week 33 · Queer ecology — these refusals are one refusal

**Historical: Rosa Bonheur** (1822–1899)
Bonheur — who wore trousers by government permit, lived openly with a female companion, and made enormous paintings of horses, cattle, and working animals — held gender nonconformity and deep ecological attention as the same inseparable practice. Her animals are not background; they are the primary subjects of a world she loved without apology.

**Contemporary: Postcommodity** (collective, formed 2007)
This Indigenous arts collective — Raven Chacon, Cristóbal Martínez, Kade L. Twist — makes work at the intersection of land rights, Indigenous sovereignty, and ecological violence. Their *Repellent Fence* stretched two miles of yellow balloons across the US-Mexico border, connecting two nations and refusing the violence of that line.

**Emerging: Raven Chacon** (b. 1977)
*Individual practice noted here alongside Postcommodity.* Chacon's solo work as a composer and sound artist holds queer ecology as sonic practice — his scores and sound installations create environments where the human and nonhuman, the traditional and the experimental, the political and the spiritual cannot be separated. He was awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Music in 2022 for his orchestral work *For Zitkála-Šá*, making him the first Native American to receive the award.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work that holds your body and the body of the earth in the same frame — not as metaphor for each other, but as genuinely entangled. Where has your body been treated like land to be controlled? Where has the land been treated like a body to be policed?

---

### Week 34 · Attention as ecological practice

**Historical: Ursula von Rydingsvard** (b. 1942)
Von Rydingsvard's massive cedar sculptures — rough-hewn, stacked, burnt, worked by hand over months — attend to the grain and weight and smell and resistance of the wood. Her practice is ecological attention as sculpture: what the material wants, what it resists, what it finally yields when held with enough patience.

**Contemporary: Andrea Polli** (b. 1968)
Polli translates environmental data — atmospheric measurements, climate records, weather systems — into sound and visual form, making ecological reality audible and visible in real time. Her practice holds the mystery of the earth's systems without resolving them into data that can be managed or ignored.

**Emerging: Nour Mobarak** (b. 1984)
Mobarak's sound installations hold attention as ecological practice — recording endangered or disappearing sonic environments, the voices of languages with few remaining speakers, the sounds of habitats under pressure. Their work asks what it means to attend to what is about to be lost with full presence. They are a Los Angeles-based artist who has exhibited at the Museum of Contemporary Art Los Angeles and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Find a material from the natural world you have not worked with before — something you can collect without harming a living system. Spend one full session doing nothing but attending to it: weight, temperature, texture, smell, sound. Make no art yet. The next day, make art from what the attending taught you.

---

### Week 35 · The Wide Waters (Phase V synthesis)

**Historical: Emily Carr** (1871–1945)
Carr's paintings of the Pacific Northwest coast — old-growth forests, totem poles, the ocean — hold the theological partnership with earth, the mystery, interbeing, and purpose as one continuous visual experience. She lived alone in a converted caravan she called the Elephant, traveling to remote coastal villages. Her practice is the wide waters as a lived commitment, not a concept.

**Contemporary: Nnenna Okore** (b. 1975)
Okore's large-scale sculptures made from jute, clay, papier-mâché, and found organic materials enact ecological interdependence as tactile fact. Her work holds the wide waters' core argument — that the human body and the nonhuman world are made of each other — in material form.

**Emerging: Jumana Manna** (b. 1987)
Manna's films and sculptures hold the wide waters as territory of contested belonging — the land, the sea, and the plant world as sites where political violence and ecological time intersect. Her film *Wild Relatives* (2018), tracing the movement of seeds from a Syrian seed bank to a Norwegian doomsday vault, is a meditation on what the earth holds that human systems cannot protect. She has exhibited at Documenta 14, the 2019 Venice Biennale, and the Serpentine Gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work about water — specifically, the water that is in your body right now. You are between 55–60% water. That water has been in oceans, in clouds, in other bodies. Make something that holds both the intimate (your body, now) and the vast (where this water has been) in the same gesture.

## Phase VI · Weeks 36–42 · The Dark Night

---

### Week 36 · Grief as sacred work

**Historical: Giovanni Bellini** (c. 1430–1516)
Bellini's many paintings of the *Pietà* — Mary holding the dead body of Christ — are not about doctrine. They are about a mother holding a body that used to be alive. His figures grieve without comfort, without resolution, without the promise that everything will be all right. He makes grief as real as the paint.

**Contemporary: Njideka Akunyili Crosby** (b. 1983)
Crosby's large paintings hold the grief of diaspora — the distance from Nigeria, the impossibility of full belonging in either place, the palimpsest of two cultures layered across a single surface. Her work understands grief not as an event but as a condition of being between worlds, held with beauty and without resolution.

**Emerging: Diana Khoi Nguyen** (b. 1985)
Nguyen is primarily a poet — her debut collection *Ghost Of* (2018), which holds the grief of her brother's suicide as an ekphrastic practice with photography, was a finalist for the National Book Award. Her practice holds grief as sacred work that exceeds genre: the photographs, the silhouettes, the text together enact what no single form can hold alone. Her work crosses between poetry and visual practice in ways directly relevant to this curriculum's hybrid methodology.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work about a specific grief — not grief in general, but one loss. Do not try to resolve the grief in the making, or to make it beautiful, or to find its meaning. Make something that holds the grief the way the body holds it: incompletely, physically, without demanding completion.

---

### Week 37 · The dark night of the soul

**Historical: Francisco Goya** (1746–1828)
*Returning from Phase IV in new territory.* Goya's *Black Paintings* — Saturn, witches, dogs drowning in sand, pilgrims in the dark — were made after he went deaf, in the isolation of his farmhouse. They are not protest; they are the dark night as private cosmology. The descent that precedes no guaranteed ascent.

**Contemporary: Glenn Ligon** (b. 1960)
Ligon's text paintings — words from James Baldwin, Zora Neale Hurston, his own voice — gradually become illegible as text accumulates, darkens, and swallows itself. His practice holds the dark night as linguistic: the moment when the language you've been given can no longer carry what you are experiencing.

**Emerging: Autumn Knight** (b. 1980)
Knight's performance and video works hold the dark night as the specific territory of Black women's interior lives — the places where the interior exceeds what external demands can contain. Her work creates theatrical situations where the performer is lost, overwhelmed, or in crisis, and asks what resources the body holds in that territory. She has exhibited at MoMA PS1, the Studio Museum in Harlem, and the ICA Houston.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work in darkness — literally. Work at night with minimal light, or with your eyes closed. Use the darkness to remove sight from the practice and attend to what the other senses carry. What does your hand know when your eye cannot correct it?

---

### Week 38 · Conscious dying and accompaniment

**Historical: Rembrandt van Rijn** (1606–1669)
Rembrandt's self-portraits across forty years — from young ambition to old, soft, mortal truthfulness — are the most sustained visual record of a human being practicing dying while alive. His late self-portraits hold the face as something always already in the process of becoming earth.

**Contemporary: Taryn Simon** (b. 1975)
Simon's long-term photographic projects — documenting the wrongly convicted, the invisible operations of state power, the things held in institutional storage — enact the contemplative practice of accompaniment: staying present to what is real, even when reality is being systematically managed out of sight.

**Emerging: Anicka Yi** (b. 1971)
Yi's olfactory and biological installations hold mortality as a material — her work uses bacteria, pheromones, decomposing organic matter, and living systems that are always already dying. Her installation *In Love With the World* in the Tate Modern Turbine Hall (2021) sent bioluminescent "aerobes" through the space as beings with brief lives, asking viewers to practice the attention that accompaniment demands. She has exhibited widely and is represented by 47 Canal and Galerie Chantal Crousel.

**Practice invitation:** Find something in your environment that is in the process of dying or disappearing — a flower past its peak, a building being demolished, a season ending. Return to it daily for a week. Let the work be a practice of accompaniment: staying present to something that is leaving.

---

### Week 39 · Non-extractive love

**Historical: Berthe Morisot** (1841–1895)
Morisot's paintings of women in domestic space — her daughter Julie, her sister, women at rest — hold attention without demand, presence without intrusion. Her brushwork is tender in the precise sense: it touches without grasping. Her practice is non-extractive love as visual method.

**Contemporary: Felix Gonzalez-Torres** (1957–1996)
Gonzalez-Torres's candy piles — the weight of the candy equaling the weight of his partner who died of AIDS — are the most precise visual definition of non-extractive love in contemporary art. The work gives itself away. You cannot possess it; you can only receive it, and in receiving it, diminish it. The diminishment is the point.

**Emerging: Doris Salcedo** (b. 1958)
*Though not emerging chronologically, Salcedo's work speaks so precisely to this territory it demands inclusion.* Salcedo's sculptures — furniture embedded in concrete, rose petals filling cracks in a plaza floor, chairs suspended on an institutional facade — enact non-extractive love as political grief. Her practice holds the disappeared, the murdered, the grieved without monumentalizing them or demanding anything in return from the viewer. She is a Colombian sculptor who has exhibited at Tate Modern, the Guggenheim, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work that gives itself away — completed by being received, touched, taken, consumed, or transformed by whoever encounters it. What does it mean to make something that does not try to survive its encounter with the world?

---

### Week 40 · The wound that has been generative

**Historical: Frida Kahlo** (1907–1954)
*Returning from Phase II.* Kahlo's *The Broken Column* — her spine as a crumbling pillar, her body pierced by nails, a medical corset — is a painting made from the darkest place of her experience that transforms, through the absolute refusal of comfort, into something that has accompanied millions of people in their own darkness. The wound is not the enemy of the work; it is its ground.

**Contemporary: Wolfgang Tillmans** (b. 1968)
Tillmans's darkroom abstractions — images made without a camera, through exposing light-sensitive paper to light directly — enact the generative wound: what looks like damage is actually the record of a direct encounter between light and surface. The most intimate possible image, made without any apparatus between the light and the receiving.

**Emerging: Kudzanai-Violet Hwami** (b. 1993)
Hwami's large-scale paintings — figures fragmented, multiplied, and reconstructed across intensely colored surfaces — hold the wound's generativity as a queer Zimbabwean visual practice. Her work understands that the body marked by exclusion has also been given, by that very marking, a particular quality of vision. She has exhibited at the Victoria Miro gallery in London, the Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago, and was included in the 2019 Venice Biennale.

**Practice invitation:** Make something from the wound that has most shaped your practice — not a work that explains the wound, but a work that demonstrates what the wound made possible. What have you been able to see, make, or offer because of what broke you?

---

### Week 41 · The purgative way

**Historical: El Greco** (1541–1614)
*Returning from Phase III.* El Greco's *The Burial of the Count of Orgaz* holds the purgative way as architecture: the earthly and the divine simultaneously present, the body being received into one order while the soul is received into another. What cannot survive love is being released while what can survive rises.

**Contemporary: Kiki Smith** (b. 1954)
*Returning from Phase III.* Smith's figures — dripping, leaking, spilling — are always in the process of releasing what cannot be held. Her work understands the purgative way as biological before it is spiritual: the body's honest acknowledgment of what must be let go.

**Emerging: Joiri Minaya** (b. 1990)
Minaya's works — which include textiles, video, and photography examining the containment and display of Dominican women's bodies in tropical imagery — enact the purgative way as the process of releasing the colonial image from within. Her *#dominicanwomengooglesearch* project and subsequent textile works hold the release of a particular kind of harm as the practice itself. She is a New York-based artist who has exhibited at the Museum of Arts and Design and the RISD Museum.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work about something you are releasing — quietly, not dramatically. Not catharsis, but the soft ongoing work of letting go. Let the work be gentle around the thing that is leaving.

---

### Week 42 · The Dark Night (Phase VI synthesis)

**Historical: Rembrandt van Rijn** (1606–1669)
*Returning from Week 38.* Rembrandt's *Return of the Prodigal Son* — the old father's hands on the returned son's shoulders, the almost unbearable tenderness of the gesture — is the dark night synthesized into a single moment of non-extractive love. Grief as sacred work, the completion of the long descent, the recognition of the human in the human: all of it in two hands on two shoulders.

**Contemporary: Kara Walker** (b. 1969)
*Returning.* Walker's *A Subtlety* — a sugar sphinx in a former Brooklyn sugar refinery, surrounded by molasses-colored children — holds grief, the dark night, and non-extractive love as a single devastating encounter with the American inheritance. It is the dark night made structural.

**Emerging: Torkwase Dyson** (b. 1973)
Dyson's paintings and sculptures hold the dark night as the specific territory of Black spatial practice — the body navigating systems built to constrain it, finding in that navigation its own geometry, its own beauty, its own way through. Her work holds the synthesis of Phase VI: grief, descent, accompaniment, and the wound that becomes generative, all held within a single abstract vocabulary. She has exhibited at Pace Gallery, the Museum of Modern Art, and the 2022 Whitney Biennial.

**Practice invitation:** Make a work in one sitting that holds as much darkness as you can hold without losing yourself. Then make a small companion work — something that fits in your hand — that holds what persists through the darkness. The large and the small together are the synthesis.

## Phase VII · Weeks 43–49 · The Queer Path

---

### Week 43 · Naming the second-satan system

**Historical: James Baldwin** (1924–1987)
Baldwin did not make visual art, but his practice of naming — saying the unsayable about race, sexuality, and American violence — is itself a visual act. *The Fire Next Time* is a work that makes systems visible. His refusal to absorb institutional violence as personal failing is the first move of the queer path.

**Contemporary: David Wojnarowicz** (1954–1992)
Wojnarowicz's paintings, photographs, writings, and films named the second satan explicitly — the Catholic Church, the Reagan administration, the medical establishment's abandonment of AIDS patients — and refused to absorb their violence as personal deficiency. His practice understood survival as the insistence on naming what was killing him.

**Emerging: Tourmaline** (b. 1983)
*Returning with specific focus.* Tourmaline's archival and film practice names the systems that have made Black trans life precarious — and refuses, simultaneously, to let that precarity be the only story. Her film *Salacia* (2019) and her archive of Miss Major Griffin-Gracy's activism hold the naming and the refusal together. Her work at the 2019 Whitney Biennial and the MoMA directly addresses the institutions that have historically excluded and harmed the communities her work represents.

**Practice invitation:** Name something — one specific institution, doctrine, or teaching that told your body it was wrong. Write the name in large letters. Make it the material of a work. Surround it with what your body actually knows to be true.

---

### Week 44 · The thesis enacts the inquiry it describes

**Historical: Claude Cahun** (1894–1954)
Cahun's self-portrait photographs — in which they dressed, performed, masked, and transformed across decades — are the most radical identity integration practice in the Western art historical record. Not a resolution of the self into a fixed form, but an ongoing practice of meeting the self in its multiplicity without requiring it to become singular. The thesis enacting its own argument looks like this.

**Contemporary: Wu Tsang** (b. 1982)
Tsang's films, performances, and installations hold the integration of trans identity, Asian American experience, and queer politics as a single, ongoing, unfinished practice. Their work understands integration not as arrival but as the practice of moving between what you have been, what you are, and what you are becoming.

**Emerging: Danielle Brathwaite-Shirley** (b. 1995)
Brathwaite-Shirley makes video game-like interactive installations that enact the inquiry they describe — asking viewers to make choices that reveal the structures of violence faced by Black trans people, while making the viewer's own position in those structures visible. The work is its own argument; you cannot experience it without the experience changing you. They have exhibited at the Serpentine Gallery in London, the ICA London, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Write the opening two pages of your synthesis section: not the argument you set out to make, but the argument that the practice has actually produced. Begin from what you have found, not what you planned to find.

---

### Week 45 · The body as theological site

**Historical: Egon Schiele** (1890–1918)
Schiele's distorted, unflinching self-portraits treat the body — his own, his lovers', the bodies of the poor and sick he drew in asylum wards — as the primary theological text. He held nothing about the body back from the gaze; he understood the body's desiring, aging, dying reality as sacred without requiring it to be made presentable.

**Contemporary: Lorraine O'Grady** (b. 1934)
O'Grady's *Mlle Bourgeoise Noire* performances — crashing white art world openings in a gown made of white gloves, flogging herself with a cat-o'-nine-tails made of white chrysanthemums — and her later photographic diptychs hold the Black woman's body as simultaneously the site of theological violence and the site of theological knowledge.

**Emerging: Cassils** (b. 1975)
Cassils's durational performance and sculpture practice holds the body as theological site through extreme physical transformation — they have trained their body in extreme conditions, cast their own flesh in plaster in real time, and made the body's materiality into the explicit subject of the work. Their *Becoming an Image* performance series and *Tiresias* video work hold the trans body as sacred text in the most rigorous possible sense. They have exhibited at MoMA PS1, the Tate Exchange, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make a self-portrait of the part of your body most subject to external judgment — most named, most regulated, most told it is wrong. Attend to it with the quality of attention you would bring to a sacred text. What does it hold? What has it survived?

### Week 46 · Two-spirit and gender-plural traditions

**Historical: Edmonia Lewis** (1844–1907)
Lewis — Chippewa and African American, the first professional sculptor of color in American history — navigated practice that refused the binary categories available to her. Her *Hagar in the Wilderness* makes a figure of exile and sovereignty from marble neoclassicism: a tradition that excluded her, inhabited by someone it had no category for.

**Contemporary: Kent Monkman** (b. 1965)
Monkman's alter ego Miss Chief Eagle Testickle — a time-traveling, gender-fluid Cree supernatural being — inhabits and rewrites European art historical canvases, posing in the settings of Ingres, reversing the colonial gaze, making queer Indigenous presence the center of the Western art history from which it was violently excluded.

**Emerging: Demian DinéYazhi'** (b. 1983)
DinéYazhi' — a Diné (Navajo) artist, poet, and curator — holds Two-Spirit identity as a living tradition rather than a historical category, making neon text works, prints, and community organizing that insist on the continuity of gender-plural sacred traditions in contemporary Indigenous life. Their neon work *I AM THE CIVILIZATION* directly addresses the colonial violence done to Two-Spirit people. They are based in Portland and have exhibited at the Portland Art Museum and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Research one specific two-spirit, hijra, or gender-plural sacred tradition. Learn enough to understand, not to appropriate. Then make a work from the quality of awareness that a tradition of sacred gender plurality might carry: the capacity to hold what appears binary as a threshold instead.

### Week 47 · Queer mysticism

**Historical: John Singer Sargent** (1856–1925)
Sargent's watercolors of male bathers in Venice and the Middle East — made for himself, rarely exhibited in his lifetime — are records of the queer mystic's private devotion: figures in water, light falling on male bodies with the same reverence El Greco brought to saints. The erotic and the mystical held in the same gaze.

**Contemporary: Harmony Hammond** (b. 1944)
Hammond's wrapped sculptures — forms bound, covered, and held together with fabric and wire — and her decades of writing and organizing for queer feminist art hold queer mysticism as a material practice. The body wrapped and tended as a form of sacred attention that neither religion nor the art world recognized as spiritual.

**Emerging: Precious Okoyomon** (b. 1993)
*Returning with specific focus on queer mysticism.* Okoyomon's garden installations hold queer mysticism as an explicitly ecological and somatic practice — the body in the garden, the garden in the body, the sacred present in what grows and what is grown by. Their practice at Documenta fifteen created a space of queer contemplative encounter with the nonhuman world. As a poet and visual artist, their work holds the mystical and the erotic and the ecological as one continuous field.

**Practice invitation:** Read one mystical text from any tradition. Pay attention not to doctrine but to the body's presence in the language. Where is the longing? Where does the language exceed the container of its tradition? Make a work from that excess.

### Week 48 · Identity integration

**Historical: Romaine Brooks** (1874–1970)
Brooks's portraits of the queer, artistic, and bohemian circles of early twentieth-century Paris — painted in severe palettes of gray, black, and white — are among the first sustained visual records of a queer world as a complete civilization: exile become community, margin become center.

**Contemporary: Naomi Rincon Gallardo** (b. 1979)
Gallardo's video performance works draw from Indigenous Zapotec tradition, queer theory, camp, and political satire to make the queer path visible as both ancient and urgently contemporary. Her work holds the full territory of the Queer Path constellation in a single practice.

**Emerging: Ashley Hans Scheirl** (b. 1956)
Scheirl — an Austrian painter and filmmaker who transitioned in the 1990s — makes psychedelic, science-fiction-inflected paintings and films that hold identity integration as an ongoing, non-linear, joyfully excessive process. Their work refuses the narrative of integration as arrival or resolution, insisting on the continued multiplicity of the self as the only honest account. They have exhibited at the Tate Modern, documenta, and internationally, and teach at the Academy of Fine Arts Vienna.

**Practice invitation:** Make five self-portraits in one session using five different modes, postures, or materials — not to play characters, but to meet five true aspects of yourself in the same session. Lay them together. The integration is not any one of them; it is the relation between all five.

### Week 49 · The Queer Path (Phase VII synthesis)

**Historical: Beauford Delaney** (1901–1979)
Delaney — Black, gay, a friend of Henry Miller and James Baldwin — made paintings of astonishing luminosity from a life marked by poverty, psychiatric crisis, and exile. His abstract works from Paris, often made in difficult material circumstances, hold the queer path's synthesis: exile become generative, the wound become light, identity held without apology.

**Contemporary: Zanele Muholi** (b. 1972)
*Returning through the full arc.* Muholi's practice holds all six stars of the Queer Path constellation simultaneously: the second-satan named, exile inhabited as power, the body as theological site, the wound's generativity, and a sovereignty so complete it has changed how the world sees.

**Emerging: Akosua Viktoria Adu-Sanyah** (b. 1993)
Adu-Sanyah's photography and installation work holds queer Black diasporic identity as the synthesis of all that the Queer Path constellation names — the wound, the exile, the naming, the body as theological site — integrated into an ongoing practice of self-witnessing. Their work creates archival and contemplative spaces for queer people of color to encounter themselves with fullness. They are a Swiss-Ghanaian artist based in Zurich who has exhibited at the Kunsthaus Zürich and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Write a short artist's statement — two pages maximum — about what you have discovered through forty-nine weeks of practice. Not what you set out to find. What you actually found.

## Phase VIII · Weeks 50–56 · The Making / Synthesis

*Note: Phase VIII weeks are primarily oriented toward thesis synthesis and writing. The artist references in these weeks are offered as companions to the writing practice, not as studio invitations separate from the thesis work.*

### Week 50 · Making as praying — art as contemplation

**Historical: Emma Kunz** (1892–1963)
Kunz made her intricate geometric drawings as healing tools, using a pendulum to guide her mark-making — process as divination, making as praying in the most literal sense. She never called herself an artist. Her work was not for exhibition; it was the practice itself, complete in the making.

**Contemporary: Richard Serra** (1938–2024)
Serra's *Verb List* (1967–68) — 84 infinitive verbs describing actions on material: to roll, to crease, to fold, to store, to bend — is itself a contemplative practice document. His process-first practice insists that what the artist does to material is as important as what results. The doing is the prayer.

**Emerging: Beatriz Olabarrieta** (b. 1977)
Olabarrieta's performance and installation practice holds making-as-praying through sustained durational acts: cooking, cleaning, singing, tending — domestic and devotional labors performed as art over long periods of time. Her work makes visible the contemplative dimension of ordinary work, insisting that the sacred is already present in the repeated gesture. She is a London-based Spanish artist who has exhibited at the Barbican, the Whitechapel Gallery, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make something this week using a single verb — one action, applied to one material, for the duration of the session. No composition, no evaluation. Just the verb and what it does.

### Week 51 · The foundations section — grounding the thesis

**Historical: Josef Albers** (1888–1976)
Albers's *Homage to the Square* series — the same compositional structure, varying only in color relationships, over decades — is the most sustained visual study of how context transforms meaning. His *Interaction of Color* is a text about foundations: not what colors are, but how they behave in relation to each other. The foundations are always relational.

**Contemporary: Howardena Pindell** (b. 1943)
Pindell's works — made from punched holes, numbered fragments, wound thread, and accumulated small marks — build up through patient, systematic accumulation into complex visual fields. Her practice understands that foundations are laid through sustained, modest, repeated acts rather than grand gestures.

**Emerging: Wendy Red Star** (b. 1981)
Red Star's practice — which combines photography, historical research, beadwork, and archival intervention — holds foundations as the work of recovering what the institutional archive has buried. Her investigations of Crow Nation history and the 1873 delegation photographs ask what a thesis grounded in Indigenous knowledge actually requires: not different footnotes, but different ground. She has exhibited at the Whitney Museum of American Art, the Portland Art Museum, and is represented by Sargent's Daughters gallery.

**Practice invitation:** Write the foundations section of your thesis — or its next draft. Let the artist examples from this week clarify what you mean by relational: that the foundations of this work are established in conversation with the stars, not prior to them.

### Week 52 · The literature section — the scholarly conversation

**Historical: Hannah Höch** (1889–1978)
Höch's photomontages — cutting and reassembling images from mass media to expose the contradictions of Weimar Germany — are practices of working through and against an existing visual language. Her cuts are not destruction; they are a method of reading the sources deeply enough to see what they refuse to see about themselves.

**Contemporary: Ellen Gallagher** (b. 1965)
*Returning from Phase II.* Gallagher's long practice of working with found imagery from Black publications — layering, adding, marking, responding — is a model of what genuine engagement with an existing literature looks like: not summary, but encounter.

**Emerging: Hito Steyerl** (b. 1966)
Steyerl's essay films and video installations hold the scholarly conversation as a form of practice: she makes arguments about image culture, capitalism, and artificial intelligence in the form of art rather than texts alone, demonstrating that the scholarly conversation can be entered through the studio as well as the library. Her works are simultaneously scholarship and art, refusing the boundary that academic writing draws around its own form. She has exhibited at the Museum of Modern Art, Documenta, and is one of the most influential artists working today.

**Practice invitation:** Identify the three scholarly sources that have most genuinely changed your thinking over this curriculum. Write one paragraph on each that holds not just what they argue but what they opened in you. This is the literature review's deepest layer.

### Week 53 · The investigation section — the practice as evidence

**Historical: Francesca Woodman** (1958–1981)
Woodman's photographs — her body blurred, disappearing into wallpaper, emerging from doorways, merging with the architecture she inhabited — are records of investigation: what happens when the self is genuinely placed at risk in the practice, when the research is done with the body, not about it.

**Contemporary: Sophie Calle** (b. 1953)
Calle's practice-based investigations — following strangers, sleeping in strangers' beds, having others follow her — produce evidence of a kind no other method could generate. Her *Suite Vénitienne* is the investigation section embodied: one body, in one practice, generating one practitioner's irreplaceable account.

**Emerging: Tavia Nyong'o** (b. 1971)
Nyong'o — a scholar and cultural critic whose work crosses between academic writing, performance, and criticism — holds the investigation section as the place where practice and scholarship cannot be separated. Their book *Afro-Fabulations: The Queer Drama of Black Life* (2019, NYU Press) enacts the kind of investigation this thesis pursues: evidence gathered in encounter with cultural practice, not prior to it.

**Practice invitation:** Write the investigation section, or its next substantial draft. Begin from the evidence your body has generated across the curriculum. What has the practice produced that the scholarship could not have produced alone?

### Week 54 · Integration — holding everything at once

**Historical: Cy Twombly** (1928–2011)
Twombly's large paintings — scrawled text, mythological reference, smeared paint, childlike mark-making, and high modernist scale — hold classical scholarship and the body's most immediate gesture in the same surface without hierarchy. His practice is integration as pictorial method: nothing has to be more important than anything else in order for the work to cohere.

**Contemporary: Kara Walker** (b. 1969)
*Returning for the final time.* Walker's practice, across three decades, holds history and body, beauty and horror, scholarship and visceral immediacy in the same cut line. Her work is the integration this curriculum has been working toward: nothing resolved, everything held.

**Emerging: Dread Scott** (b. 1965)
Scott's practice — which has held the full arc of Black American political life across four decades, from his 1989 *What is the Proper Way to Display a US Flag?* to his recent *Slave Rebellion Reenactment* project — holds integration at the level of history and body, the personal and the structural, in the same gesture. His work understands that the thesis of this curriculum — that the ordinary body is the site of the sacred — is inseparable from the political history of which bodies have been treated as ordinary. He has exhibited at the Brooklyn Museum, MoMA, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make a visual work this week alongside the thesis writing — not to illustrate the thesis but to occupy the same territory from the studio side. Let both the writing and the making be active simultaneously. Notice what each knows that the other doesn't.

### Week 55 · The finished thesis is also unfinished

**Historical: Paul Cézanne** (1839–1906)
*Returning from Phase V.* Cézanne's late works — many left ostensibly unfinished, with raw canvas showing through — are complete in a way that "finished" paintings sometimes are not. His incompletions are honest: the mark stops when the perception stops, not when convention requires closure. The unfinished is sometimes the most truthful.

**Contemporary: Theaster Gates** (b. 1973)
*Returning.* Gates's practice never concludes; it accumulates. The Stony Island Arts Bank is not finished; it is ongoing. His practice understands completion as a stage in a longer process, not as the termination of the inquiry.

**Emerging: Tourmaline** (b. 1983)
*Returning for the final time.* Tourmaline's archival and film practice is always in process of becoming — the archive keeps arriving, the story is never complete, because the lives it documents are not finished. Her understanding of the thesis as unfinished is the most rigorous possible: the inquiry she holds is alive, ongoing, and refuses to be sealed into a fixed argument.

**Practice invitation:** Write the section of your thesis that acknowledges what you could not find, what the practice opened but could not fully traverse, what questions remain genuinely alive. This is not weakness. It is the most accurate thing the thesis can say.

### Week 56 · You are already made of the thing you thought you were seeking

**Historical: Mark Rothko** (1903–1970)
*Returning for the final time.* Rothko's Chapel in Houston was his last major work — sixteen paintings made for a non-denominational space of contemplation, in which color is stripped back until what remains is the barest presence. He died before the Chapel opened. The work is an ending that is not a conclusion: a threshold, held open.

**Contemporary: Cecilia Vicuña** (b. 1948)
*Returning for the final time.* Vicuña's quipus — made from the discarded, the fragile, the materials no one kept — are offerings without guarantee. Her practice has, across six decades, insisted that the work of tending what is barely here is the most important work. *You are already made of the thing you thought you were seeking.*

**Emerging: Kapwani Kiwanga** (b. 1978)
Kiwanga's installations — working with light, color, architectural space, and the history of plants used in colonial and resistance contexts — hold the final week's claim in its most precise form: you are already made of what you seek. Her *Flowers for Africa* series, in which she recreates floral arrangements from photographs of African independence ceremonies, holds the sacred in the ordinary without sentimentality. She is a Canadian artist of Tanzanian heritage who won the 2020 Turner Prize and has exhibited at the Centre Pompidou, the Serpentine Gallery, and internationally.

**Practice invitation:** Make one final work — not as conclusion, not as summary, not as proof. Make something that holds where you are, in this body, at the end of fifty-six weeks of practice. Then begin again.
